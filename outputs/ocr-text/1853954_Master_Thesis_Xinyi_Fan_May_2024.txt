Abstract
With the escalating global attention on climate change and environmental concerns, electric
vehicles (EVs) have emerged as a crucial solution for mitigating CO2 emissions, driving a
surge demand worldwide. As the leading EV manufacturer and having solidified its leadership
in the Chinese market, BYD has accelerated its expansion efforts overseas. However, its entry
into the German market with electric passenger vehicles at the end of 2022 has yielded
disappointing sales over the past year. Thus, this master thesis aims to explore How can BYD
adjust its business strategy adapting to the German EV market to increase sales? The
analysis addresses three sub-questions: the institutional opportunity and challenge does BYD
face through the lens of institution-based view, the opportunities and challenges BYD face in
the German EV market is investigated by applying Porter’s Five Force and VRIO frameworks
is used to identify the resources can help BYD achieve a competitive advantage in the German
EV market. Throughout the analysis, key considerations for BYD's strategic adjustment in
Germany include enhancing infrastructure with government collaboration to address
shortcomings in current EV policies, boosting brand awareness and loyalty, maintaining its
R&D advantage to develop cutting-edge technologies while ensuring cost control, and
adopting market segmentation strategies to tailor objectives and marketing approaches for
different segments. These adjustments are crucial for BYD to expand its customer base and
increase sales in the German EV market.




Keywords: Electric vehicle, BYD, Business strategy ,Electric vehicle, Institution-Based
View, Porter’s Five Forces, VRIO framework.

1.3 Key definition


1.3.1Competitive advantage
Porter and Barney both have contributed to the concept of "competitive advantage"
significantly. According to Porter "competitive advantage" is the capacity acquired through
distinctive attributes and resources to outperform others within the same industry or market.
(Porter, 2008)
Barney has described "competitive advantage" as a company deemed to possess a competitive
advantage when it is executing a strategy that creates value and is not being pursued by any
current or potential competitors at the same time. The competitive advantage may be
temporary and can be eroded over time as competitors imitate or surpass the company's
strategies. (Barney, 1991)


1.3.2Sustained competitive advantage
Sustained competitive advantage represents a lasting form of superiority in the market,
enduring over an extended timeframe. It entails upholding a leading position through
elements that are challenging for competitors to duplicate or counteract, such as proprietary
technology, robust brand allegiance, or exclusive access to crucial resources. This enduring
advantage empowers a company to sustain its dominance and profitability over the long haul.
(Barney, 1991)


In this thesis, the competitive advantage pertains to BYD's internal resources and
competences. Through the VRIO examination, BYD’s internal resources and competences in
which could be considered as the sustained competitive advantage is identified. This is pivot
for BYD increase EV sales in Germany.

2.4 Challenges of German EV Market
However, many German scholars and media regarding the electric vehicle industry are
currently not as optimistic as Statista. The journal” Prioritizing the Chicken or Egg? Electric
Vehicle Purchase and Charging Infrastructure Subsidies in Germany” described that the effect
of the German electric vehicle industry on reducing CO2 emissions is not obvious, because
the continued growth of total private vehicle demand has kept the conversion rate between
fossil fuel vehicles and electric vehicles stable, and the proportion of coal-fired power is still
high at 38% in 2018. This once again confirms that the current penetration of the German
electric vehicle market is insufficient. The key reasons involve capital investment and cost
control. The first is that the development of the electric vehicle industry requires a large
amount of capital to promote technological innovation and improve urban infrastructure.




                                                                                               21
These investments are often long-term investments that will not pay off in one or two years.
Although European governments have developed incentive schemes for electric vehicles
(European Alternative Fuels Observatory, 2020), as this involves changing the structure of
charging infrastructure, fossil fuel stations are now spread across the country. With the
transition to electric vehicles, in Germany, the grid not entirely decentralized, the scarcity of
infrastructure in remote areas will also affect the speed of the electric vehicle transition. (Zink,
Valdes, & Wuth, 2020)




The journal also points out that the transition to electric vehicles hinges on advancements in
battery technology. Currently, Germany lacks a competitive edge in battery production, as
Bosch is the only German company in engaged in this technology who already decided

3.1 Institution-Based View (IBV)
As globalization accelerates and multinational corporations expand, corporate management
faces novel challenges, necessitating a global outlook to formulate effective strategies. The
institutional landscape of economies and the significance of institutional evolution play
crucial roles in the strategic management of multinational corporations. Adopting an
institution-based perspective is pivotal for analyzing and discussing enterprise strategic
development within the broader system.


According to Richard W. Scott, institutions are frameworks and practices shaped by rules,
norms, and cognitive systems that uphold societal stability and imbue social behavior with
meaning. (Scott, 2013) Building upon previous scholarship, Mike W. Peng refined the



                                                                                                25
institutional framework, delineating its scope in which encompasses both formal and informal
systems governing individual and corporate behavior. (Meyer, Estrin, & Bhaumik, 2008)
Formal systems encompass legal statutes, regulations, and similar structures, while informal
institutions encompass norms, cultural values, ethics, and the like. These institutions are
supported by three main pillars: regulative, normative, and cognitive.


The regulative pillar primarily involves the coercive authority of governments, serving as a
cornerstone of the institutional framework. Regulation involves establishing rules,
monitoring compliance, and implementing consequences or incentives to influence future
actions. (Scott, 2013)


The normative pillar encompasses a prescriptive aspect that examines how the beliefs, values,
and norms of relevant stakeholders influence the conduct of individuals and organizations.
From a sociological standpoint, common values and beliefs are often prevalent within
religious systems, social classes, business groups, and communities. (Scott, 2013) In his book
"Institutions and Organizations: Ideas, Interests, and Identities," underscores the comparison
between "appropriateness" and "instrumentality" as highlighted by scholars. When
considering normative issues, the fundamental question shifts from "What choice will yield
the best outcomes?" to "Given my role and the specific context, what behavior is most suitable
for me to adopt?" (Scott, 2013)


The Cognitive Pillar, as defined by Scott revolves around the shared perceptions that define
the essential characteristics of a social system and establish its interpretive frameworks. Scott
emphasizes the "cultural-cognitive" label within his analytical framework to underscore how
external cultural influences shape internal interpretive frameworks. Cultural systems have the
capacity to influence cognition, permeate social values, and construct societal norms across
various levels, ranging from the formation of social groups to the operational logics of
organizations and the institutional patterns of national governance. (Scott, 2013) While




                                                                                              26
cultural-cognitive elements are widely recognized as consistent components in diverse
systems, their degree of institutionalization often varies, leading to ongoing debates and
negotiations during social reform and development processes. ( DiMaggio & Powell, 1983)

internalizing the values and beliefs that are taken for granted, mainly focusing on informal
institutions. With the existence of these three pillars, institutions can influence the behavior
of individuals and enterprises. (Garrido, Gomez, & Juan, 2014)

3.3 Resource - based view, VRIO framework
Resource Based View is one of tradition perspective of strategy management focus on firm-
level and describing and identifying the internal strength and weakness of the company and
it is a managerial framework use to determine the strategic that can exploit to achieve
competitive advantage. The roots of RBV found during the 90th, several scholars point it out.
However, this paper focus on the interpterion of resource-based view introduced by Jay
Barney in 1991. According to the definition of Barney, (Barney, 1991) resources include all
assets, skills, technologies, organizational characteristics, and knowledge owned by the
enterprise.


Resource-Based View emphasizes that a company's competitive advantage comes from its
unique and difficult-to-imitate resource combination. These resources enable firms to provide
value creation, scarcity, non-substitutability, and organizational capabilities to achieve
sustained competitive advantage. RBV believes that corporate strategies should be dedicated
to developing and utilizing these unique and valuable resources to create lasting competitive
advantages. In addition, Barney also proposed two assumptions that underpin sustained
competitive advantage. Firstly, resource heterogeneity suggests that firms within the same
industry possess distinct bundles of resources, leading to varying levels of success. This
implies that certain firms excel over others due to their unique resources. Secondly, resource
immobility refers to resources that are challenging to trade or replicate. This assumption is
also known as a VRIO framework that is a pivot measurement tool on whether resources can
provide enterprises with sustainable competitive advantages. (Barney, 1991)




                                                                                            31
The resources could be divided to threshold capabilities and distinctive capabilities.
Threshold resources refer to the basic resources and competencies required to enter and
compete in an industry. The distinctive resource are those resources that can enable an
organization to achieve competitive and the distinctive resources require ownership and
control of resources and competencies that separate a business from the rest. VRIO refers to
the four characteristics of Value (valuable), Rarity (rare), Inimitability (difficult to imitate)
and Organized (organized). Only when resources or capabilities possess these four
characteristics, they can consider as the sustained competitiveness advantage of an enterprise.
(Barney, 1991)

5.4.1 Blade battery


BYD officially introduced the Blade Battery in March 2020 that utilizing lithium iron
phosphate technology installed in the       BYD HAN model initially. The Blade battery
innovatively skips the module assembly process, significantly improving volume utilization.
This design allows for fitting more battery cells within the same space, increasing energy
density by over 50% compared to traditional battery packs.




BYD's Blade Battery has undergone rigorous safety tests, including the "Mount Everest" of
battery safety tests – the nail penetration test (Apendix2), and extreme stress tests such as a
46-ton heavy vehicle rollover test. It boasts exceptional safety, strength, range, and lifespan.
Safety features include seven-dimensional safety testing covering internal and external short
circuits, overcharging, collisions, high voltage, connections, and hazardous gases.
In comparative tests conducted by BYD, the Blade Battery outperformed NCM Lithium and
cubic lithium iron phosphate batteries in safety. After nail penetration, the Blade Battery
remained unharmed, with no fire or smoke observed on the surface, and a surface temperature
of 30-60°C. In contrast, NCM Lithium batteries experienced severe burning with surface
temperatures exceeding 500°C, while cubic lithium iron phosphate batteries charred with
observed smoke and surface temperatures ranging from 200-400°C. (BYD, BYD blade
battery safer, better, stronger and more reliable, 2024)

5.4.2 E-platform
BYD's E-platform technology is a four-motor drive system capable of precisely controlling
the dynamic of all four wheels, enabling safe driving in extreme conditions such as high-
speed tire blowouts, emergency floating, and icy roads. Additionally, the technology enhances
off-road capabilities. In terms of electronic control, the E-platform equips all models with a
new generation SIC, boasting a high computational efficiency of up to 99.5% and control
speed, increasing current output capacity by 50% for precise control of current output to all
four driving wheels. On the perception level, the E-platform technology integrates various
sensors, including traditional real-time data and intelligent driving sensor data such as
cameras, LiDAR, millimeter-wave radar, and high-precision positioning. These sensors
enable rapid and accurate reception of signals, supporting the identification, decision-making,
and control of the four motors.


To achieve deep integration of perception, the E-platform technology utilizes a highly
coordinated control architecture between the central computing platform and domain
controllers. Through real-time exchange of perception information and control strategies via
high-bandwidth, low-latency, and highly secure in-vehicle Ethernet, precise and diverse
control of the four motors is achieved. As a result, the E-platform technology platform can
independently adjust the dynamics of all four wheels at millisecond speed through a stronger
perception power of the electric drive and electronic control system. This improves vehicle
stability, reduces the risk of loss of control due to insufficient traction, and enhances handling
stability, providing safety assurance for various usage scenarios. (Westerheide, 2024)

BYD DMI (Dual Mode Intelligent System) Technology is an efficient electronic control
system serving as the dual-motor drive system for BYD vehicles. DMI Techonology offers

high-speed driving. Overall, BYD DMI Technology provides low fuel consumption, high
efficiency, and an exceptional driving experience. The Core Components including the EHS

recommendation focus on strengthen the construction of charging infrastructure, enhancement
of brand awareness and loyalty, diversify the brands and product portfolios and develop
different target on different market segments aim to focus on the core segment and product
accelerate BYD's customer expansion and increase the sales. However, the German EV markis
dynamic and competition is intense, in which presents both opportunities and challenges. BYD
needs to continually adjust its strategies to keep pace with market changes to succeed.

Firstly, if the EV market continual to drop, how should BYD deal with it? As mentioned in

electrification, resulting in a 10-14% drop in electric vehicle sales this year. German
companies such as Mercedes-Benz will continue to research internal combustion vehicles in
the next 10 years. If this situation continues, it will have a huge negative impact on the BYD’s
EV development in Germany.
