Introduction
Brand storytelling has become a focus of marketers in recent years, and with good reason.
In a survey of 2,000 adults by marketing agency Headstream, 79% indicated that they
want brands to tell stories in their marketing, but 85% could not give an example of
a memorable brand story they had seen (Mortimer, 2015). Consumers are now awash in
branded ‘clutter’ found in online ads, videos, blogs, and other outlets; all of it demanding
attention, but much of it lacking in added value. A recent study from media agency Havas
found that 84% of consumers expect brands to produce content such as videos, podcasts,
or experiential events (Vizard, 2017). Yet, the same study found that 60% of consumers
rate this content as poor quality or irrelevant.
   Consumers prefer narratives over factual arguments (e.g., Adaval & Wyer, Jr., 1998;
Escalas, 2007; Polyorat et al., 2007), but marketers struggle with creating stories that are
both engaging and effective at driving business outcomes. Brand storytellers must find
new ways to gain the attention of consumers and make an impression. One effective way
to grab attention is to use incongruent elements in the story that counter expectations,
which research does not adequately explore.

    The expectations we have for stories derive from schemas, or semantic knowledge
structures, encoded and stored in our memory over time as we experience storytelling
(DeRosia et al., 2011; Mandler, 1982; Sujan & Bettman, 1989). When a new story is
encountered, schemas are accessed. Comparisons and judgments are made, attitudes
are formed, and new schemas are encoded in relation to what was already known.
A similar process occurs in branding with brand schemas, which hold our knowledge
and expectations for brands (Arbouw et al., 2019; Bhaduri & Ha-Brookshire, 2017; Halkias,
2015).
    Brand storytellers must be aware of story-related schemas held in consumers’ minds if
they are to tell effective stories. This is true if they want their stories to follow conventional
norms and meet expectations, or if they wish to surprise the consumer by defying
storytelling norms (e.g., Phillips & McQuarrie, 2010). Straightforward stories that align
with consumer expectations can certainly be effective. But in the fight over consumer
attention that is happening today, these stories risk appearing bland and predictable. If
marketers wish to defy expectations and become unpredictable, they must introduce
elements to the story that are incongruent with the schemas held in consumers’ minds.
Incongruity creates tension, which leads to more complex and deliberate processing in
the minds of consumers (Lee & Schumann, 2004). More complex processing can lead to
narrative transportation (Green & Brock, 2000), which produces more impactful story­
telling (Van Laer et al., 2013).
    The objective of this research is to propose a new way to conceptualize brand story­
telling and improve effectiveness by introducing elements of incongruity. But before
a marketer can break storytelling norms and defy expectations, it is important to first
understand these norms. Stories are usually structured chronologically, with a beginning,
middle, and end (Escalas, 2004). Stories have elements that can be identified, such as
characters, setting, plot, theme, conflict, and resolution. We expect these elements to take
familiar shape, or fit a predetermined structure, depending on the type of story being told
(Heckler & Childers, 1992; Maille & Fleck, 2011). Storytellers manipulate these elements to
achieve different effects. In the case of brand storytelling, marketers are able to strategi­
cally manipulate these elements to achieve brand outcomes such as a change in attitudes
or beliefs.
    Storytelling is a way to convey meaningful ideas, and it can act ‘as a clear aid to
memory, as a means of making sense of the world, as a way to make and strengthen
emotional connections, and as way of recognizing and identifying with brands of any
type’ (Herskovitz & Crystal, 2010, p. 21). People naturally think in terms of narratives rather
than arguments, and in that sense the human brain may be hard-wired for narratives
(Jameson, 1981; Schank, 1990; Schank & Abelson, 1995; Turner, 1998). Storytelling has
a natural place in marketing, as the human impulse to seek out narratives and symbols
that help form identity may be core to the idea of branding (Hirschman, 2010).
Accordingly, storytelling can drive positive attitudes toward the brand (Coker et al.,
2017), foster feelings of trust with clients and decision makers (Anaza et al., 2020;
Gilliam & Flaherty, 2015) and create an enhanced customer-brand experience (Lemon &
Verhoef, 2016; Lundqvist et al., 2013).
    Despite the importance of storytelling and the benefits of countering expectations,
little research exists on either the strategic manipulation of story elements or the use of
incongruency in brand storytelling. These gaps in knowledge leave marketers guessing as
                                                      JOURNAL OF STRATEGIC MARKETING        1265


to why their brand stories sometimes fail to connect with consumers. Managers have little
guidance for how to adjust the storytelling process to improve marketing outcomes. For
storytelling research to have managerial relevance, these gaps need to be addressed.
Thus, this research seeks to answer the following research questions:

RQ1: What effect do incongruent story elements have on narrative processing?

RQ2: Could incongruity positively impact storytelling outcomes related to the brand?

RQ3: How do situational or message factors impact the effectiveness of storytelling
incongruity?

   This research proposes a model for enhancing storytelling effectiveness with the
strategic and specific use of incongruity to enhance the likelihood of narrative transporta­
tion. First, the storytelling process and the role of schema incongruity in brand storytelling
are discussed. A conceptual model is proposed, including moderating factors that could
dampen or enhance the effectiveness of brand storytelling. Guidelines are offered for
manipulating specific story elements to achieve incongruity. Examples of brand stories
using incongruence are provided to further aid implementation. Ideas for future research
in this space are also proposed.


Schema incongruity and storytelling mechanics
A schema is a semantic knowledge structure that allows us to make sense of new
information and experiences in the world based on what we have already learned and
experienced (Anderson, 1988; Beals, 1998; Yoon, 2013). When a new experience occurs,
schemas related to that experience are triggered. The new input is compared to these
schemas and encoded to memory in relation to what is known (Beals, 1998).
   When new information about an object (i.e., a brand, product, or ad) can be easily
categorized by existing schemas, it is schema congruent information (e.g., a Tesla ad
touting the benefits of electric cars). In contrast, when new information does not easily
relate to existing schemas, it is schema incongruent (e.g., a Tesla ad featuring a car flying in
space). The level of incongruence is determined by the degree to which the new
information conflicts with existing schema or expectations (Halkias & Kokkinaki, 2013,
2014; Meyers-Levy et al., 1994). Different levels of incongruity trigger different levels of
processing in the mind (Mandler, 1982).
   Information containing little or no incongruity does not require complex processing.
Any incongruence would go unnoticed, and the message may not gain the attention of
the receiver. In contrast, moderate levels of incongruity requires more complex proces­
sing in the receiver’s mind. This processing triggers either an attempt to accommodate
the new information with existing schema, or the development of an alternative schema
to categorize the new information. Accommodation can be successful or unsuccessful,
leading to different affective responses. Severe incongruity requires a high level of
attention and complex processing and is unlikely to be understood by all receivers. The
result is often unsuccessful accommodation of the new information and a negative
reaction. In cases of severe incongruity, the highly incongruent elements can lack any
1266      D. M. HOUGHTON


sense of relevancy. That is, the elements detract from, rather than contribute to, identi­
fication of the message in the story (Heckler & Childers, 1992; Maille & Fleck, 2011).
    Empirical studies show that moderate levels of incongruence produce more favorable
evaluations than either congruence or extreme incongruence (Meyers-Levy & Tybout,
1989). Halkias and Kokkinaki (2013, 2014) document the presence of an ‘inverted U’
relationship between the levels of ad-brand incongruity and positive outcomes such as
recall and attitudes. Moderate incongruity is more likely to be accommodated successfully
(Lee & Schumann, 2004; Mandler, 1982). This could result in either a positive or negative
response, but the response is more likely to be based on the information presented and
not an inability to process the incongruity. The lesson for marketers is clear: Too little
incongruity can produce easily processed but unremarkable messages, while too much
incongruity can confuse consumers and produce negative reactions. However,
a moderate amount of incongruity can draw the attention of the consumer, trigger
complex processing, and generate responses to the branded communication with little
risk of the message being lost on the consumer.
    Incongruity can take many forms, depending on the elements in question. Similarity or
dissimilarity can occur between objects according to physical characteristics or imagery,
the sensations or emotions elicited by the objects, the time and space in which the
objects exist, the manner in which the objects are used, and more (Maille & Fleck, 2011).
The storytelling process and story elements provide a multitude of objects and qualities
that can be set apart from each other through dissimilarity to produce incongruity.


Narrative transportation
Complex processing is key to effective storytelling because of the mechanics of how
storytelling works. Narrative transportation, or absorption into the story, is the process that
separates stories from other forms of influential communication. Through narrative trans­
portation, stories engage our senses and produce experiences similar to those in the real
world (see Paul, 2012 for a review of the neurological research). Narrative transportation can
generate vivid mental imagery and produce both affective and cognitive responses, leading
to changes in beliefs, attitudes, intentions, and behavior (Dunlop et al., 2010; Kim et al., 2016;
Van Laer et al., 2013). As a result, consumers identify with the characters in a story and attach
meaning to the brands and products used (Cooper et al., 2010).
   Transportation first requires attentional focus on the imagery and affect portrayed in
a story, which incongruity can achieve. As discussed, attentional focus leads to complex
processing, which is required for the receiver to be transported. The stronger the trans­
portation effect, the more likely the receiver will form favorable evaluations and adopt
story-consistent beliefs (Green & Brock, 2000). Narrative transportation can occur with
narratives based on either fact or fiction, which is good news for marketers who frequently
blend the two for brand storytelling. Transportation can also cause consumers to gloss
over ‘false notes’ in the story that may ordinarily trigger disengagement and counter­
arguing (Green & Brock, 2000).

Brand story schema
Prior marketing research assumes that consumers use only brand schemas to evaluate
brand communications. Alternatively, consumers may use schema held for the story­
telling medium itself to judge brand stories. Consumers have years of experience with
stories. Just as they hold expectations (and schemas) for brands and products, they also
hold expectations (and schemas) for the storytelling process. We expect stories to follow
the familiar pattern of a linear, chronologically ordered narrative with a clear beginning,
middle, and end (Escalas, 2004). Stories feature one or more characters, a setting, and
a plot (i.e., goals, conflict, and resolution) (Lamb, 2008). The specific makeup and structure
of these elements can vary based on the story genre, the writing style of the author, and
other factors. For the purposes of this research, the following story elements are examined
within brand storytelling: character, setting (time and location), and plot (goal, challenges,
solution). Elements of a brand story encountered by consumers will either agree (be
congruent) or disagree (be incongruent) with the schemas consumers hold for these
elements.


Conceptual model
This research proposes a model of brand storytelling that explains how brand story
schema incongruity can impact narrative processing and ultimately brand outcomes
(See Figure 1). Storytelling elements provide imagery and produce affect, two of the
necessary inputs for narrative transportation (Green & Brock, 2000). Factors related to the
message and the message source (i.e., brand) attenuate the likelihood that story elements
will drive narrative transportation. Story elements can also impact attention, the third
prerequisite for transportation. Individual and situational factors affect the likelihood that
attention will activate narrative transportation. Brand outcomes will be impacted based
on the effectiveness of narrative transportation.

Level of incongruity
The level of incongruity between new story elements and the existing story schema will
determine whether complex processing takes place in the mind of the receiver (Mandler,
1982). Low levels of incongruity do not trigger complex processing, which is necessary for
narrative transportation. High levels of incongruity can prevent the receiver from accom­
modating the new information, preventing processing from taking place. In the cases of
both high and low levels of incongruity, narrative transportation is unlikely. Moderate
levels of incongruity lead to complex processing, and thus an increased likelihood of
narrative transportation (e.g., Halkias & Kokkinaki, 2013, 2014). To account for the inverted
U relationship, two related propositions are offered:
   P1a: Low or high levels of incongruity in story elements will decrease the likelihood of
   narrative transportation.

   P1b: Moderate levels of incongruity in story elements will increase the likelihood of narrative
   transportation.

While the level of incongruity can affect the likelihood that the story elements will trigger
narrative transportation, incongruity also impacts attention level, which is a primary driver
of narrative transportation. Increased levels of incongruity are more likely to gain the
attention of viewers (Germelmann et al., 2020). But unlike the previous relationship, there
is no evidence of an inverted U here. Thus:
   P2a: Higher levels of incongruity in story elements will increase the amount of attention paid
   to the story and the likelihood that narrative transportation will occur.

   P2b: Lower levels of incongruity in story elements will decrease the amount of attention paid
   to the story and the likelihood that narrative transportation will occur.



Narrative transportation and brand outcomes
Narrative transportation requires imagery and affect (delivered by the story) and the
attentional focus of the receiver. This triggers complex processing that immerses the
receiver in the story both cognitively and affectively, often producing vivid mental images
(Green, 2004). Transportation can lead to the receiver disengaging from existing attitudes
and beliefs and updating them based on what is conveyed in the story (Van Laer et al.,
2013).
   Through this process, narrative processing can create and enhance self-brand connec­
tions, helping to incorporate the brand into a consumer’s self-concept and produce
favorable attitudes (Escalas, 2004). Transported individuals are more likely to perform
story-consistent behaviors such as buying a product or taking better care of themselves
(Van Laer et al., 2013). Additionally, there is evidence that both narrative transportation
and schema incongruity can lead to better recall of a message (Shen & Chen, 2006; Van
Laer et al., 2013). Thus:
   P3: Narrative transportation will lead to:

   (1) better brand recall,
   (2) increased likelihood of adopting story-driven beliefs,
                                                          JOURNAL OF STRATEGIC MARKETING            1269


  (3) positive attitude to the brand,
  (4) positive attitude to the story,
  (5) behavioral intentions in line with the message, and
  (6) increased favorability of the brand.


Moderating factors
Prior knowledge/brand familiarity
Familiarity that comes from having prior knowledge or personal experience with a story
topic increases the likelihood of narrative transportation (Van Laer et al., 2013). An
individual’s familiarity with the brand may also help incongruent elements stand out,
requiring complex processing. Evidence suggests that the use of incongruity may be more
worthwhile for familiar (vs. unfamiliar) brands (Dahlén & Lange, 2004). Thus:
  P4: High levels of familiarity with the brand elements in the story will increase the likelihood of
  narrative transportation occurring.


Cognitive load
Processing narratives (especially narratives containing incongruence) requires cognitive
resources. When consumers are under heavy cognitive load, they are less likely to be
transported (e.g., Chang, 2009). Consumers may experience heavy cognitive load when
dealing with multiple inputs at the same time, such as a large number of ads a website.
Stories placed in this environment are unlikely to lead to narrative transportation. Thus:
  P5: High cognitive load will decrease the likelihood that the attention given to the story will
  be sufficient to drive narrative transportation.

Narrative transportation can be an effective method for persuasion (e.g., Escalas, 2007).
However, consumers may possess persuasion knowledge, or beliefs about the motives
and tactics used by marketers to persuade consumers (Campbell & Kirmani, 2000; Friestad
& Wright, 1994). This knowledge forms over time as consumers interact with salespeople
and evaluate marketing messages. This is important because inferences made about
a marketer’s attempt at manipulation will affect perceptions of the brand and its message,
leading to adverse outcomes (e.g., Boerman et al., 2017; Germelmann et al., 2020; Grigsby
& Mellema, 2020; Wentzel et al., 2010).
   Online brand storytelling may elicit persuasion knowledge due to how it is promoted.
Social media content is less likely to produce narrative transportation if the consumer is
informed that the content is advertising due to the influence of persuasion knowledge
(Seo et al., 2018). Many brands publish their stories as sponsored or promoted posts on
social media, often through celebrity endorsers, influencers, or other brand partners. The
‘sponsored’ label conveys that the message is an advertisement, which can make manip­
ulative intent salient and cause consumers to draw on their persuasion knowledge
(Boerman et al., 2017). Native advertising, another outlet for brand stories, can also elicit
persuasion knowledge (Amazeen & Wojdynski, 2019; Grigsby & Mellema, 2020; Jing Wen
et al., 2020). If a consumer draws upon persuasion knowledge, it will negatively impact the
ability of the brand story to drive positive outcomes, whether or not narrative transporta­
tion occurs. Therefore:
1270      D. M. HOUGHTON


   P6a: Activation of persuasion knowledge will negatively affect the ability of narrative trans­
   portation to drive positive brand outcomes.

   P6b: Activation of persuasion knowledge will negatively affect the ability of schema incon­
   gruity to drive positive brand outcomes in the absence of narrative transportation.



Discussion
Storytelling has the potential to be an effective method of persuasive communication for
brands. Most consumers today expect brands to tell stories, but few stick in their memory
(Mortimer, 2015). Brand storytellers must find new ways to make an impression.
Introducing elements to a brand story that are inconsistent with consumers’ expectations,
or schemas, is likely to grab the attention of consumers. This research develops
a conceptual model and propositions that incorporate schema incongruity, narrative
transportation, and moderating factors to explain the effectiveness of brand storytelling
and guide brand storytellers toward favorable outcomes.


Theoretical implications
This research offers significant contributions to marketing theory. The proposed rela­
tionships between story elements, incongruity, and narrative processing in the concep­
tual model (Figure 1) tie together disparate areas of research within brand
communication – schema incongruity and narrative processing. Incongruity with
schema draws attention and triggers cognitive processing in the mind of the story
receiver (Lee & Schumann, 2004). Attentional focus is necessary for narrative transporta­
tion, which occurs through cognitive processing (Green & Brock, 2000). Linking these
two communication concepts makes sense and represents a significant step in brand
storytelling research.
   Additionally, the notion of brand story schema is a worthwhile and prudent extension
to brand communication research (e.g., Dahlén & Lange, 2004; Herskovitz & Crystal, 2010).
Brand storytelling research is in a nascent stage, and marketers need to account for
consumers’ storytelling expectations moving forward. Otherwise, a significant portion of
brand storytelling outcomes may go unexplained. Third, the idea of story schema is a new
concept to storytelling research. Storytelling is ubiquitous in today’s society, yet the
author could find no research examining the encoding, storage, and retrieval of story-
based schema. Consumers expect stories to follow a certain format, and this expectation
comes from schema developed over a long period of time.


Managerial implications
This research is driven by a desire to give marketing managers methods for improving
brand storytelling effectiveness. Thus, a number of managerial contributions can be
drawn from this paper. First, this research identifies the importance of incongruity in
drawing attention to brand stories. Consumers are awash in marketing content today, and
countering consumer expectations through the use of incongruity is an effective way for
brands to make an impact.

    Brand story schema incongruity can aid brand managers in accomplishing strategic
goals. For example, the Anheuser-Busch water canning ad demonstrates that incongruity
can be used to show the public a different side of the company, one aligned with
consumer expectations of corporate responsibility. The Tesla customer story about car
safety highlighted an unknown benefit of Tesla’s vehicles that represents a major factor in
consumer car buying decisions. Incongruity can also introduce new products to consu­
mers (e.g., Subway breakfast sandwiches) and draw new potential customers to the
company’s products (e.g., Minnetonka Moccasins). Incongruency is a means for upending
expectations, which brands often seek to do.
    Additionally, this research breaks down the individual elements of a story and demon­
strates how brand managers can manipulate each element to achieve incongruity. The
characters, setting, and plot all offer opportunities to introduce incongruent elements. For
example, a character that acts against the brand persona can appeal to new target
consumers. The time and location for the story can signal when and where a brand’s
products are usually used. By changing one of these elements, brands can expand the use
scenarios of its products. Marketers would be well-served in learning the elements
involved in storytelling and how to construct a story to achieve specific ends. Examples
of brand stories using incongruity are included to aid both understanding and application
(see Table 1 and Appendix A). In addition, this research offers specific recommendations
for implementing schema incongruity into storytelling elements (below).

Recommendations for implementation
In order to introduce incongruence into brand storytelling, one or more of the storytelling
elements outlined in Figure 1 must be manipulated to be incongruent with consumer
expectations. See Table 1 for a summary of these elements and current examples of
brands using this technique. Below is a short discussion of each major element.

Character incongruence
The character in a story is one of the first elements people perceive. The main character of
a brand story is often a typical customer of the brand or the easily recognizable and
memorable brand persona – ‘the articulated form of the brand’s character and person­
ality’ (Herskovitz & Crystal, 2010, p. 21). In the case of a customer character, incongruence
is accomplished by introducing a character that is unlike the typical customer of the
brand. This could be part of a strategic move to pursue under-tapped consumer seg­
ments. One example is the Ariel laundry soap ad in India that encouraged dads to
‘#ShareTheLoad’ around the house and help with the laundry (Appendix A).
    The brand persona provides a consistent point of reference for consumers across brand
assets and represents the brand’s values to the world. A strategic deviation from an
established brand persona would be an impactful form of incongruence that would
make a strong impression. However, introducing incongruence directly with the brand
persona is risky and would likely require a longer-term approach accompanied by
a strategic change within the firm.

Setting: time incongruence
The setting of a story (i.e., time and place) is crucial to establishing context. In brand
storytelling, time can be considered in two ways: the time in which a consumer encoun­
ters a brand and its products, and the broader time period in which the brand exists in
history. Introducing incongruence to the time of use shifts the context people use when
thinking of the brand. A good example is the Subway Breakfast ad, which introduced
customers to new morning hours and breakfast items (Appendix A). The ad features
a disheveled character named Bruce who ‘is not designed to operate at 7 AM.’ One
morning, Bruce stumbles into Subway ‘at the right time.’ The breakfast sub and coffee
combo snaps Bruce out of his daze.
   It is also possible to set a brand story in a time period not associated with the brand.
This construal of time is more difficult to accomplish and comes with added costs such as
                                                    JOURNAL OF STRATEGIC MARKETING       1273


period staging and dress, and perhaps special effects. An example of this form of incon­
gruence is the medieval knight ad campaign by Bud Light (Appendix A) that highlights
the use of corn syrup in competitors’ beer. The medieval setting draws viewers in and
grabs their attention.

Setting: location incongruence
The location of a story is vital to framing the other story elements. Consumers expect to
encounter brands in certain locations but not others, which makes location a prime target
for incongruity. A story posted by Minnetonka Moccasins (Appendix A) details a hiking
adventure undertaken with the brand’s moccasins, including pictures taken at stops along
the hike. Moccasins are usually sold for leisure use in the U.S., with a focus on style and
comfort. Displaying them in a lounge or bedroom would be congruent with expectations.
Taking them on a hike may cause consumers to reconsider the product’s characteristics.

Plot: goal incongruence
Plot is the engine that drives the story. A plot contains various elements, but this research
focuses on the goal, challenge, and solution. A character strives to achieve a goal (i.e.,
fulfill a want or need). For example, a mom tries to get her kids to school on time. In
a brand story, the brand and its products are tied closely to the goal. To introduce
incongruence, the storyteller needs to associate the brand with a goal not usually sought
by the brand’s customers (or not usually fulfilled by the brand). This could change the way
people think about the brand and its place in their lives.
   An example is the Care Counts campaign by Whirlpool to address student dropout
rates (Appendix A). The video features interviews with people like 4th grader Vanessa, who
says ‘When I wake up in the morning and I find out I have no clean clothes, I usually end
up just staying home.’ Another student said that when his family was homeless, they had
to spend their money on food and not clean clothes. Whirlpool installed washers and
dryers in schools to allow students to do their laundry and attend class without shame.
Whirlpool has always been associated with doing laundry, but not with a goal such as
keeping kids in the classroom.

Plot: challenge/struggle incongruence
When a character pursues a goal, the storyteller introduces tension to the story by placing
challenges, or obstacles, in front of the character that they must overcome to achieve the
goal. For example, a mom tries to get her kids to school on time, but breakfast takes too
long. In congruent stories, the challenge rings true for the brand’s customers. In an
incongruent story, the challenge is one not familiar to the brand’s typical customers. An
example is the Maltesers chocolate ad in the U.K. (Appendix A). The ad features a disabled
actress describing a romantic encounter that was almost cut short by her disability.

Plot: help/solution incongruence
As the character pursues the goal and struggles to overcome challenges, help emerges in
the form of a brand’s product. The obstacles are overcome, and the character accom­
plishes the goal. The mom can now get her kids to school on time with the help of
Kellogg’s breakfast bars. In congruent stories, brands play to their known strengths and
demonstrate how their products can help people accomplish their goals. In stories with an
1274       D. M. HOUGHTON


incongruent solution, the audience is shown a new side of the brand. An example is the
Anheuser-Busch water canning ad (Appendix A). Everyone is familiar with the AB brand,
but few people know they ship cans of water to hurricane relief sites, where clean fresh
water is scarce. Another example is the Tesla customer story about a severe car accident
that left the driver unscathed. People do not usually associate Tesla with safety.


Future research
The topic if incongruity in brand storytelling is rife with future research possibilities. First, with
the help of technology, consumers can now be active participants or co-creators in brand
storytelling (Lund et al., 2018; Singh & Sonnenburg, 2012). Consumers may naturally introduce
incongruence into their stories, since their experiences with a brand will be diverse, unpre­
dictable, and personal. Customer stories are unlikely to mimic the ways in which the brand
portrays itself, and they will be seen as more authentic. Future research should examine the
effectiveness of customer-introduced incongruity and how brands can harness it.
   Second, the topic of strategic incongruity in storytelling can be further pursued. Tying
storytelling elements and forms of incongruity to strategic marketing goals is the next
logical step. For example, if a firm wanted to introduce a new product feature to an
existing market to solve an emerging consumer problem, what would be the best way to
highlight this feature using incongruity? How would this be different than the solution for
introducing a new-to-the-world product? Answering these questions would require
researchers to map the methods for achieving incongruence onto known strategy issues.
Such an endeavor would be highly useful to marketers.
   Third, while this research makes a strong case for the use of incongruity in storytelling,
future research needs to empirically investigate the relationships proposed in the con­
ceptual model. Experimental research exists on the use of incongruity in advertising (e.g.,
Dahlén & Lange, 2004; Halkias & Kokkinaki, 2013, 2014), however no such studies exist for
brand storytelling. Specifically, experiments should examine the impact of incongruity
when used in each storytelling element. It is likely that some elements are more effective
agents of incongruity than others. Additionally, brand, product, and industry factors will
impact the effectiveness of incongruity in storytelling. For example, it is unclear if
technology brands would benefit from placing the story out of time with the technology
featured in the story. Doing so may have unintended consequences such as the technol­
ogy being perceived as dated by the consumer.
