                        Department of Communication and Media

     Through analysis of five focus groups with people who “usually (more often than
     not) listened to at least one podcast episode a week” in the last two months, this
     uses and gratifications (U&G) study uncovers several prominent themes among
     podcast listener experiences. In an effort to integrate old and new media use
     typologies within U&G research, our results are presented in two parts: emerging
     typologies and expansions of existing typologies. The emerging section includes
     reasons for podcast displacement of other media, customizable experiences, and
     multitasking (which involves both temptation bundling and feeding the brain). The
     expansion builds on the existing typologies of companionship and interaction by
     analyzing avenues for parasocial relationship formation and identifying social
     opportunities as well as social frustrations surrounding podcasts. We ultimately
     conclude that podcasts offer an endless supply of engaging content that travels with
     listeners, allowing them to be “productive” in various physical and mind-expanding
     ways. The enjoyment is facilitated in part by forging connections with hosts and
     other listeners.

We invite them into our kitchens while we cook, our cars while we commute, our
offices while we work, and our pockets while we clean. A portmanteau of
broadcasting and iPod (McClung & Johnson, 2010), podcasts are audio files
that can be downloaded or streamed from a mobile device, usually at no direct
cost to the user. This unique distribution avenue has enabled audio media
innovation spanning topic, time, and structure. We consider it safe to say that
podcasting will never be a peak mass medium, but, as Berry (2016) asserted, it
appears to be entering a “period of credibility, stability and maturity” (p. 668).
Indeed, statistics from the 2017 Edison Research study on podcasts revealed that
40% of the representative U.S. sample had listened to a podcast and 24%
engaged in monthly listening, showing steady incremental growth in both cate-
gories over the past 5 years, from 29% and 14%, respectively (“Podcast
Consumer,” 2017). Scholars have described podcast engagement as a “niche
prosumer activity” (Menduni, 2007, p. 16), and noted that intense, loyal audience
engagement is prized over size (Wade & Patterson, 2015). Podcasting seems to
have found and stabilized its entertainment identity emphasizing a more personal
and personalized experience.
    Surveying the podcast literature, Markman (2015) highlighted the paucity of
audience research, specifically on audience habits and motivations. This uses and
gratifications (U&G) study works to close that gap in the literature by finding
answers to this overarching research question: What specific U&G are listeners
seeking and obtaining from podcasts? Answers to this question will help us more
clearly see what this medium is offering to its increasing number of audience
members, thus capturing emergent typologies for new media gratifications. The
process of answering this question will also help us make methodological
recommendations to other U&G researchers. Sundar and Limperos (2013)
recommended “using the classic two-step [U&G] methodological approach of
focus groups followed by surveys” (p. 507). The authors opined, however, that
U&G researchers have tended to skip the first step, moving swiftly to survey
designs that feature “instruments from studies of older media, modified slightly
to suit the particular medium under investigation” (p. 507). The current research
will hold heuristic value for scholars interested in conducting U&G-based survey
research by providing a set of testable gratifications uniquely applicable to the
podcast medium.
    Through analysis of five focus groups with people who “usually (more often
than not) listened to at least one podcast episode a week” in the 2 months prior to
the focus group, this study uncovers several prominent themes among podcast
listener experiences. Our results are presented in two parts—emerging U&G
typologies and expansions of existing typologies—to offer a clear dialogue with
the existing body of U&G literature. The emerging section includes reasons for
displacement of other media, customizable experiences, and multitasking (which
involves both temptation bundling and brain food). The expansion builds on the

enjoyment is facilitated in part by forging connections with hosts and other

                        U&G LITERATURE REVIEW

U&G has been variously referred to as a theory, a perspective, and an approach
to understanding people’s media use (Palmgreen, 1984). Here it is perhaps best
thought of as “a way of thinking about and empirically testing individuals’
motives for media use” (Krcmar & Strizhakova, 2009, p. 64). The U&G tradi-
tion, regardless of the medium under investigation, is predicated on the assump-
tion of active audience members who might use, interact with, and be impacted
by that medium in various ways dependent, at least in part, upon the users’
individual differences (Sundar & Limperos, 2013). U&G, then, foregrounds a
general conception of media users as discriminating, selective, rational, and in
possession of enough self-awareness that they can identify the needs they seek
from their media diet (Ruggiero, 2000). By their very nature, podcast users are
noteworthy for “active decision making” as they “actively search for content”
(Berry, 2016, p. 666) and interact with other users in a social multimedia
environment (Markman, 2015); therefore, the assumption of an agentic, purpose-
ful audience makes good theoretical sense in the current investigation.
   Central to the U&G approach is the practice of categorizing various media
user needs into one or a number of related typologies (Wang & Tchernev, 2012)
that capture gratifications or “perceived fulfillment[s]” of those needs
(Palmgreen, 1984). For example, Rubin (1984) divided the needs associated
with TV viewing into either instrumental or ritualized. Rubin’s media use
dichotomy has since been applied to media engagement behaviors such as talk
radio listening (Rubin & Step, 2000), Internet use (Sundar & Limperos, 2013),
and multitasking using various media (Wang & Tchernev, 2012). There is a long-
standing practice dating back to the 1970s for U&G researchers to slot needs and
gratifications into existing media use typologies, such as emotional, cognitive,
social, habitual, or affective (see, e.g., Krcmar & Strizhakova, 2009; Schramm &
Hartmann, 2008; Wang & Tchernev, 2012) and to integrate established U&G
with emergent gratifications from newer media (e.g., Ferguson & Perse, 2000;
Papacharissi & Rubin, 2000).
   Ruggiero (2000) asserted that sorting media needs into typologies does not
provide a “refined theoretical perspective,” but it does offer “a benchmark base
                                            PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY            99

of data for other studies” (p. 12). On the other hand, Krcmar and Strizhakova
(2009) cautioned that the proliferation of typologies without an associated
attempt to integrate them “may do little to forward uses and gratifications as a
meaningful approach” (p. 56). Sundar and Limperos (2013) argued that U&G
researchers need to make space for more specific and emergent gratifications
rather than relying only on established, generalized, and aggregated gratifications
from older media if scholars want to improve their chances of accurately making
sense of the role media use plays in today’s society. Ruggiero struck a productive
middle ground by suggesting that U&G scholars take a hybrid approach by using
theoretically relevant elements of existing gratification typologies while also
making conceptual and empirical space for emergent needs associated with
newer media practices. Our work aligns with this middle ground as it presents
the analysis in a way to specifically highlight emergent gratifications and build
out established typologies.
    So how might we define and situate the podcast medium in the assemblage of
media options that came before it and emerged along with it? Wade and Patterson
(2015) clarified, “Podcasting is neither limited to nor defined by its technologies.
Rather, it is a specific set of practices and cultural meanings that are entirely
entwined with the technologies for its distribution, organization, and consump-
tion” (pp. 221–222). Here, Wade and Patterson tap into one of the more difficult
filigrees to untangle using U&G, namely, how a given technology’s affordances
bump up against and comingle with users’ preexisting individual differences as
they use that technology within a particular sociocultural context. Eloquently
explicating the antecedent, mediating, and consequent factors (Rubin, 1994)
associated with early podcasting, Berry (2016) analyzed the iPod’s radio-
related history, technological invitation to users (which includes the iPod and
earbuds), and early commuter context, noting that these factors promoted “a
privatized and personalized auditory experience” (p. 664).
    Of course the gratifications associated with personalized podcast listening did
not emerge out of thin air but rather developed in a complex process of
displacement of and convergence with older media. From its roots in broadcast
radio, to its immersive auditory older cousin audiobooks or books on tape
(Markman, 2015), to the debt it owes to the mobile audio technologies like the
Walkman, MP3 players, and smartphones (Berry, 2016; Wei, 2008; Zeng, 2011),
it becomes clear that podcasts already have a foundation in U&G research.
    Berry (2016) identified overlapping and distinctive U&G between radio and
podcasting, arguing that listening to traditional radio is more of a linear process
with messages moving mostly from sender to receiver, whereas podcast listening
involves the choice to create a social yet personalized and customizable listening
experience. Numerous other gratifications have been identified in the U&G
literature related to podcasting and the technologies that afford them. These
gratifications include sharing content through social media (Menduni, 2007),

time shifting (McClung & Johnson, 2010; Wade & Patterson, 2015), place
shifting (Wade & Patterson, 2015; Wei, 2008), parasocial relationships with
podcast hosts and characters (Hartmann, 2008), listener control and choice
(which overlap with time and place shifting; Albarran et al., 2007), entertainment
(McClung & Johnson, 2010), relaxation (Albarran et al., 2007), and passing the
time (Albarran et al., 2007). Our study digs deeper into emergent U&G, many of
which focus on the ability to enhance one’s productivity by multitasking with the
podcast medium. This multitasking theme represents an enhancement of the
“control” U&G typology (of selecting the medium and content one wants,
when one wants it). Users are not just controlling the listening experience:
They are better able to control what else they are doing while they are listening.


                                    METHODOLOGY

After securing Institutional Review Board approval from Merrimack College on
March 1, 2017, a team of two professors and two directed study students
conducted focus groups that spring. Participants were recruited through flyers
posted on the campus of a midsize private New England college, e-mails to
campus constituents, word of mouth, and the researchers’ social media accounts.1
Two groups were conducted in person on the college campus, and three groups
were conducted virtually (Stancanelli, 2010) using Google Hangouts. All parti-
cipants received $10 Amazon gift cards.
   The groups were segmented according to the recruitment method and the
recruiter. For example, the two students primarily recruited participants from
campus for in-person focus groups held in a library meeting room. Almost all of
their participants (N = 9) were students, staff, or faculty. The focus groups
conducted online (N = 14) comprised the two professors’ personal contacts,
social media connections, and snowball sampling (e.g., some participants invited
a friend or coworker). The online focus groups tended to attract more avid
podcast listeners who were eager to speak with others about their experiences,
whereas the face-to-face participants tended to be more casual listeners. The
researchers intentionally avoided a student-only sample and a sample recruited
through podcast fan communities. The convenience sample and the online focus
groups were used in an effort to maximize geographic and age diversity, as well
as capture a wide variety of listening preferences and purposes.
   After reviewing and signing consent forms (in person or through e-mail),
participants filled out a brief questionnaire that asked about demographics, as
well as podcast listening information: how, how much, and what podcasts they

   The study’s single-spaced transcripts collectively took up 62 pages. The
transcripts were analyzed using grounded theory. Following grounded theory
procedure (Corbin & Strauss, 2015), the first author read through all transcripts
carefully without taking notes, took notes during a second reading, and created a
transcript memo on a third reading that listed out all possible themes (46 total).
The most notable themes were selected based on their frequency of mention and
the level of detail focus group participants provided about the theme. At that
point, the second author added new support to the memo and weighed in on what
themes should be covered in the analysis. In the end, we settled on five themes to
present a full picture of the U&G participants found in podcasts: reasons for
media displacement, customizable experiences, multitasking (with “temptation
bundling” and “feed the brain” as gratification subthemes), parasocial relation-
ship pathways, and social opportunities/frustrations. The multitasking section,
covered in the middle of the analysis, represents what we see as the most unique
gratification focus group participants were taking from podcast listening.


                                      ANALYSIS

To both embed our findings in previous U&G typologies and to make space for
emergent findings, the analysis is organized into two main sections: (a) emergent
gratifications and (b) expansions of established gratifications. Our findings in the first
theme—media displacement—illustrate the interaction between technological affor-
dances and listener agency (see, e.g., Sundar, 2008). The media displacement theme
implicitly taps into the changing relevance and availability of particular typologies,
along with the expected gratifications those affordances may nurture. Our second
major section—expansions on previously established gratifications—helps put our
findings in dialogue with existing typologies, demonstrating deeper understanding of
parasocial relationship formation and the social opportunities/frustrations associated
with podcast listening.


                         EMERGENT GRATIFICATIONS

Media Displacement: Fresh and Mobile
Focus group participants’ descriptions of their pathways to podcasts—and the media
they transitioned from—help us understand how podcasts offered emergent (or
resurrected) U&G in particular contexts. Podcasts were most commonly replace-
ments for radio/music and television. They seemed to be most similar to (and, for
some participants, on a continued rotation with) audiobooks. Marian (White woman,
37) saw differences between contemporary radio and podcasts but similarities to
                                                PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY               103

radio as it once was, noting that podcasts hearken “back to like old-timey radio. So
that’s part of what appeals to me about it is listening to stories on the radio.” Studying
radio U&G in the medium’s golden age, Herzog (1944) observed that radio story-
telling enabled listeners to “fill in gaps of their own life” (p. 24). Facing television
competition, radio shifted focus from stories to music, but it seems that people are
craving audio raconteurs once again. Jessica (White woman, 30) listened to audio-
books for at least 5 years before participating in the focus group. She found audio-
books and podcasts to offer a similar experience, stating, “I’ve always been attuned
to storytelling formats. Podcasts were just newer.”
    “Boring” and “repetitive” were the most prevalent criticisms of contemporary
radio and music. Ryan (Asian American man, 35) recalled turning to podcasts in
2009 or 2010 because he “was just kind of bored at work” and tired of “the same
music over and over.” Lily (White woman, 42) did not like the thought of going
back to listening to the radio once she became a podcast convert because “I’d
probably be pushing the buttons a lot because there’s nothing on that I would
want to hear.” Even switching to satellite radio was not enough to satiate Dan’s
appetite for new content. Dan (White man, 55) was an early adopter of satellite
radio and felt it “was starting to get a little played, a little boring” when he
decided to switch over to podcasts. Both Lily’s and Dan’s preferences and
expected gratifications changed along with audio media evolutions.
    In contrast to “boring” radio, podcasts were seen as fresher, more unique, and more
engaging in part because of their rapidly expanding content. Wendy (White woman,
35) agreed with Ryan’s assessment of music being the “same stuff over and over” and
appreciated podcasts as part of her steady media diet because “there’s always some-
thing new.” The extensive catalogs of old podcasts and newly released episodes from
various genres enable listeners to perpetually find something unique at their leisure. Jeff
(White man, 39) noted that the wide availability of different podcasts led him to crave
fresh content, a relatively permanent shift in his listening preferences: “I like listening
to shows I’ve never heard before. [Listening to podcasts] has changed what I want.”
Jeff’s language about a shift in what he wants relates to Sundar and Limperos (2013)
observation that an emergent gratification (in this case, craving unique and nonrepe-
titive content) may become a more stable gratification. This gratification may even
transcend the medium.
    Whereas radio was too boring and repetitive, television was engaging—but not
mobile enough. Despite being able to watch television content on portable screens,
the need to view the screen was too restrictive for our podcast listeners. This theme
really took off in one focus group, starting with Alan (White man, 39):

   I used to sit and watch TV constantly and that’s a lot harder now with the family
   growing. … So there’s always something to do at the house, and I can get it done
   more easily if I’m listening to a podcast. And I can’t get it done at all if I’m trying
   to watch TV.

A smartphone playing a podcast can be tucked into one’s pocket as one moves
about the house putting away laundry, making lunches, cleaning, or completing
other tasks. Joel (White man, 50) concurred with Alan, preferring podcasts
because “you’re up and mobile. You’re not locked into being in front of a TV,
and you can, you know, do whatever you gotta do.”
   We find a contradictory pair of themes under the media displacement
umbrella: Podcasts can facilitate both productivity and relaxation. Gwen, Peg,
and Colin would listen before bedtime to unwind. Gwen (White woman, 44)
switched away from watching TV in the evening and instead used podcasts “to
decompress in bed.” Peg (White woman, 26) thought that podcasts were an
excellent “TV replacement, especially for maybe like later at night, trying to
avoid like having screens on or around.” For Colin (Chinese man, 25), podcasts
afforded an unusual combination of stimulation and relaxation: “It’s like my
bedtime story. … If I can just close my eyes, I can just fall asleep while still
getting some information.” The auditory, rather than visual, stimulation offered
Colin a chance to both learn something and pave the way for sleep.



Customizable Experience
Offering consumers an easily customized experience is a burgeoning media trend:
from search engine alerts, to on demand content, to streaming services. Albarran
et al. (2007) applied the term “demassification” to describe the most recent era in
audio entertainment in which users are asserting power over the medium to select
particular content at particular times. Peg (White woman, 26) described the agentic
appeal of podcast listening this way: “I think I might be like outing myself as a
millennial but the best thing about podcasts for me is that I get to choose what I
listen to when I listen to it and how I listen to it.” Colin (Chinese man, 25), another
millennial, was also emphatic when praising the personalized experience: “It’s anti-
TV programming where somebody is … telling you you’re going to watch CSI on
Thursdays. … I’m like, ‘What kind of mood am I in? I’m going to listen to this.’”
He later summed it up as, “I curate my own experience.” Both Wendy (White
woman, 35) and Marian (White woman, 37) appreciated podcasts because you can
just “skip around” episode to episode.
    Jeff’s (White man, 39) explanation for the appeal of customization references
not just personal enjoyment but efficiency: You have “limited time in your day,
limited time in your in your, you know, life, and you can only experience so
many things. So I think it’s a good opportunity to listen to what you really want
to listen to.” Creating one’s own listening experience from all of the available
episodes dovetails with multitasking and a desire for efficiency. In various ways,
podcasts allow listeners to squeeze the most out of every moment.

Multitasking
We define multitasking simply as listening to podcasts and engaging in at least
one more activity at the same time (see also Jeong & Fishbein, 2007; Wang &
Tchernev, 2012). Multitasking can be motivated by several factors, including
perceived efficiency, enjoyment, and habit (Hwang, Kim, & Jeong, 2014). This
theme relates to Lull’s (1980) classification of television as an environmental
resource, a companion—for accomplishing things like chores. As this theme
demonstrates, however, podcasts are seen as a better companion than television
for many tasks.
    Presenting our results Family Feud style reveals that commuting or driving
was the most popular activity to pair with podcast listening (n = 15), followed by
doing chores (n = 12), bathing (n = 5), working (n = 4), dog walking (n = 4),
getting ready for the day (n = 3), and using social media (n = 3).6 Commuting
was not only the most popular activity to go with podcasts, it was often what
drove people to podcasts.7 Wendy (White woman, 35), one of our earliest
adopters, said she “started listening in 2009 on my way to and from my first
job out of school,” which involved “an hour train ride.” Peg (White woman, 26)
used This American Life “to kind of break up my commute because I was biking
about 12 miles a day and I needed something to do with my time.” Peg was
already doing several things with her time—traveling to work and exercising—
but she wanted further stimulation and enrichment.
    This idea of making better use of one’s time (through multitasking) was quite
common in interviewees’ descriptions of their behaviors. Joel’s (White man, 50)
dialogue captures that sentiment: “I have an hour commute to work so … I’ll
listen to a podcast and try to educate myself in some way or whatever, make it
more productive than just sitting in traffic.” The word “productive” was a
common descriptor of the experience of multitasking with podcasts. Listening
was a way to expand one’s world while physically doing something else. When
asking friends for podcast recommendations, Rachel (White woman, 37) phrased
it this way: “What should I do with my ears while I’m driving?”
    And what do you do once you have arrived at work? Listen to more podcasts
was the answer for some. Three of four interviewees who specifically mentioned
listening at work noted an affinity for “background noise.” For example, Ryan
(Asian American man, 35) stated, “If I’m at work, I also like to have noise in the
background so sometimes I’ll listen there as well.” Ambivalent attitudes emerged
in this thread. Wendy (White woman, 35) described her habits this way: “In the

    The previous two paragraphs offer unique looks at arousal regulation. Whereas
mood management theory explains how individuals optimize affect and arousal
(Zillmann, 1988) and mood adjustment accounts for how people regulate affect
and arousal in preparation for upcoming tasks (Knobloch, 2003), the regulation
taking place here occurs simultaneously with the multitasking activity. We call this
multitasking mood management “mood balancing” because the nature of the
podcast is complementary to the nature of the work. For folks doing boring
tasks, the podcast was an engaging arousal boost. For those at work who perhaps
liked background noise but still needed mental space to think, the podcast was
more of an arousal moderator.
    As a counterpoint to the multitasking section, it is imperative to note that
some interviewees stated that they did not multitask while listening to some or all
podcasts. Grace (White woman, 21) commonly multitasked, but it was not her
only way to listen. She recalled, “There are times when I will sit down and focus
strictly on the podcast.” Some interviewees were highly focused on a specific
type of podcast, such as Jessica (White woman, 30), who noted that if it is a
“hard reporting” podcast, “that’s definitely much more of like a just listening
kind of thing” compared to a “junk foody” type of podcast where you “don’t
really have to pay that close of attention.” Podcasts, like many other forms of
entertainment, are flexible and able to be to be used for deep dive into a story or
background noise that can be easily tuned in or out.

   Temptation Bundling. Multitasking describes a behavior that comes with
several gratifications: feelings of productivity, learning something new, and
passing the time. Feelings of productivity can spring from (a) getting a work or
household task accomplished and/or (b) learning or expanding one’s world through
a podcast. We got a taste of this theme when focus group participants compared
podcasts to other media. As we develop the multitasking theme more thoroughly
here, we see that podcast listening should be considered a form of temptation
                                                  PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY                 107

bundling. Temptation bundling, a concept that the first author heard about through
the Freakonomics podcast, involves coupling “instantly gratifying ‘want’
activities” with engagement in a ‘should’ behavior” that is not immediately
gratifying and requires willpower to complete (Milkman, Minson, & Volpp,
2014, pp. 283–284).8 If you have to do something mundane, distasteful, or
boring, make it more enjoyable by putting on an engaging story or learning
something new about the world. This temptation bundling offers what we see as
bundled or tandem gratifications.
    Ryan (Asian American man, 35) explained the appeal of multitasking when
doing something mundane this way: “It kind of just makes me feel like I’m not
doing what it is I’m doing.” This distraction is particularly welcome when one is
engaged in an unpleasant task. Rachel’s (White woman, 37) example highlights
this point: “Head lice has been going through my daughter’s classroom. So
the hour that we sit combing and picking, she gets to choose her podcasts and
that makes it slightly more bearable.” Jessica (White woman, 30), who listened
while shoveling snow, used similar wording, saying that podcasts “made [shovel-
ing] more bearable, so like the couple of times when I didn’t [listen to podcasts] it
was definitely like, ‘I’m never gonna be done.’” Podcasts cannot just help a person
get started on a boring or mundane task, they can also help motivate one to see the
task through and make time feel like it is passing more quickly. Podcast listening
could therefore be seen as a high-tech version of whistling while you work.
    Listening to storytelling podcasts in particular seemed to help some partici-
pants lose track of time. Jenny (White woman, 36) offered support for the
temporal warp speed, explaining, “I think the other reason why I prefer [pod-
casts] to listening to music is like the time goes by quicker when I’m listening to
a story.” Although the researchers did not focus on storytelling, quantitative
findings by Chinchanachokchai, Duff, and Sar (2015) affirmed that media multi-
taskers perceive time as passing more quickly and experience more enjoyment
than those only engaged in one task. Dan (White man, 55) described his love for
stories at several points in the focus group, noting that National Public Radio’s
StoryCorp was what initially drove him to download podcasts. He knew he
found a great story if this happened: “I won’t be able to get anything else done
until I hear the end of the podcast. And so I will sit in my car, in front of my
house just so I’ll be able to listen to it.” Several of Dan’s fellow focus group
members nodded in agreement. Lily (White woman, 42) from a different group
stated, “Sometimes I’ll just drive out of my way so I can have more time to
listen.” Even if podcast listening’s boring multitasking companion (e.g., com-
muting) ceased, the podcast listening would often continue.

          EXPANSIONS OF ESTABLISHED GRATIFICATIONS

Pathways to Parasocial Relationships
Many participants described their relationships with podcast hosts using lan-
guage that signified parasocial relationships, which are defined as the “seeming
face-to-face relationship” between audience member and performer (Horton &
Wohl, 1979, p. 32). Discussing Pop Culture Happy Hour, Jenny (White woman,
                                            PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY            109

36) said, “I love the people. I want to be best friends with them all.” Jeff (White
man, 39) considered his relationships with hosts to be like a “friendship” or
“fondness.” Peg (White woman, 26) went as far as saying, “Ira Glass feels like
one of my friends.” Addressing the mostly one-directional nature of parasocial
relationships, Jessica (White woman, 30) described podcast hosts as “friends that
don’t know they’re my friends.” Parasocial relationships were a driving force in
many interviewees’ decisions about what to listen to. Rebecca (White woman,
32) stated, “Most of the podcasts I listen to are actually the ones that have hosts
where I feel like I could be friends with them in real life.”
    Interviewees were able to provide not just evidence of parasocial relationships
but explanations about why they formed those relationships, the latter of which
expands on the companionship U&G typology. This typology covers a sense of
being around (mediated) others (see, e.g., Papacharissi & Mendelson, 2007;
Rubin, 1983). Media companionship can include parasocial relationships, lone-
liness mitigation, or other feelings of sociability. The most common themes were
the frequency or regularity of contact, opportunities to interact with hosts through
social media or other avenues (thus creating the possibility for a two-way
relationship), the conversational quality of the podcast, similarities between
listener and host, and host sharing of personal information.
    Jeff (White man, 39) was fond of Roman Mars, host of 99% Invisible,
“because of the frequency in which I listen to [the show].” Several interviewees
were able to increase their exposure to hosts by using social media—and some-
times have a true interaction. Wendy (White woman, 35) followed “pretty much
all” of the podcast hosts she listened to on Twitter and sent story ideas to some.
Grace (White woman, 21) often multitasked by looking at Twitter to see other
listeners’ podcast reactions and to tweet at hosts. She enthusiastically admitted,
“Sometimes I hope that they’ll even read my tweet!” This mechanism for listener
to host backchanneling was important to Rachel (White woman, 37), who
e-mailed her focus group moderator about it on April 12, 2017: “No other
medium I consume has a mechanism for listeners to comment directly and/or
direct the content. … Somehow the podcast feels more intimate, more personal
for this solicitousness.” This solicitousness offers the mutualistic benefits of
drawing listeners further into the podcast world, thereby encouraging loyalty
and a more enjoyable, engaging experience.
    Having several hosts or panelists weigh in on a topic helped some listeners
feel like there was a space for them in the conversation. Ryan (Asian American
man, 35) explained it this way: “If you’re just listening to one person it feels as if
you’re being talked to as opposed to those types of [podcast] conversations. … I
almost feel a little bit like I’m a part of it in a way.” The dialogic nature of some
podcasts, including the sports ones Ryan listened to, helped to invite listeners in
with their room for dis/agreement and space for multiple voices. Jenny (White
woman, 36) stated that Pop Culture Happy Hour “feels like the kind of
110      PERKS AND TURNER


conversation I would want to have with a group of my friends,” and she reacted
accordingly, “pretend[ing] to be part of that conversation” and sometimes talking
to the hosts “even though I’m in my car and they can’t hear me.” This type of
participatory response is a sign of Jenny deriving enjoyment from the seemingly
interactive nature of the program.
    Host sharing of personal information sometimes cultivated this sense of
intimacy. There was an element of uncertainty reduction in this exchange (see
also Rubin & Step, 2000). Elizabeth (White woman, 54), a West Wing Weekly
fan, noted that the hosts “talk a lot about different things in their personal lives so
I do feel like I have a sense of their personality as distinct from the content of the
podcast.” Grace (White woman, 21) listened to Always Open, a podcast that bills
itself as a “late-night chat” with friends. She mentioned that the hosts “share
about things that are personal like the first time they had sex. … These personal
stories aren’t things that people just share unless you really get to know them. …
It forces you to feel close.” Hearing a host’s personal experiences and having
experiences in common can enhance those feeling of connectedness. Rebecca
(White woman, 32) stated that listening to podcasts “makes me feel less alone,
knowing that other people have these experiences or are going through similar
struggles.” The personal topics of conversation, the dialogue among hosts (or
among hosts and guests), and commonalities between host and listener can all
present invitations for intimacy.
    Surprisingly, these host connections were powerful enough to keep some
interviewees listening to new episodes—even if the topic was not appealing.
Alan (White man, 39) mentioned Lauren Spohrer from Criminal as a favorite. He
noted there’s “something about her voice and her delivery. … I’m just always
excited when it comes on, even if I don’t care about the topic.” Joel (White man,
50) admitted to the same practice, remarking, “I’ll sit through podcasts that I’m
not as interested in or something, the subject matter that might not be as
interesting, just because I like the host.” Jessica (White woman, 30) cited Last
Podcast on the Left as an example in which she might not be “terribly interested”
in a given episode topic but she “like[s] the people enough to just stick with it
anyway.” This theme suggests a host sincerity/listener loyalty connection that is
consonant with what Horton and Wohl (1979) posited in their initial work on
parasocial relationships.


Social Gratifications/Frustrations
Listening to podcasts was often described as an individualized experience, but
one that was enveloped in social practices, such as recommending podcast
listening to others, recommending specific podcasts or episodes, or discussing
the content of an episode. Because communication with others often preceded
and followed podcast listening, it has some commonalities with the social
                                                        PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY   111

interaction U&G typology (see, e.g., Papacharissi & Mendelson, 2007).9 The
multitasking aspect of podcast listening often contributed to the solo nature of the
experience: Many focus group participants were listening while commuting,
cooking, and cleaning—activities that are often completed alone. It was also
difficult for some to coordinate tastes with others. Ryan (Asian American man,
35) noted simply, “If my wife is with me [while driving], we always listen to
music because we don’t really share the same interests in terms of podcasts.”
Gwen (White woman, 44) recounted a longer struggle with her wife that began
with talk radio, which Gwen “loves” and her wife “hates”: “I’ll find myself
listening to things and I’ll say, ‘Oh my gosh, there’s this really great podcast and
you have to listen.’ … And she’ll be like ‘No, I’m not doing this.’” Perhaps the
experience of an intimate podcast is all one needs to feel connected. Rebecca
(White woman, 32) enjoyed listening to podcasts alone in part because “it fills up
the empty space with something new … something more … emotional? It gives
me more emotional and intellectual fulfillment.”
    Recommendations from others were often precursors to this solo media
engagement experience. Both Marian (White woman, 37) and Lily (White
woman, 42) had robust recommendation exchanges with their work colleagues.
Marian recommended several titles through her weekly work newsletter. Lily
described a workplace social life in which podcasts were a common topic of
conversation: “We talk about the content. We recommend to each other because
we know [what] we all like. We talk about how fun it would be to do a podcast.”
For Jenny (White woman, 36), it took unique content to get podcast-related
conversations going: “Serial was probably the big one that got me started like
actually having conversations with people about podcasts.” Laura (White
woman, 35), too, was engaged in discussions about and even had a listening
party for Serial in which her group of friends “just hung around the iPhone and
listened to [a final Serial episode] while we ate pizza.” Laura’s Serial group
reactivated to recommend and discuss S-Town.
    Despite these examples of recommendations and discussions, several podcast
listeners expressed confusion or frustration regarding others’ unwillingness to
listen to podcasts or to understand the medium. Peg (White woman, 26)
explained, “Something that I find really interesting is the kind of general
hesitancy around downloading the app and figuring out how to get to podcasts
and start listening to them.” Wendy (White woman, 35) found it frustrating that
her parents who are avid National Public Radio listeners would not listen to
podcasts: “I’ve even, I’ve downloaded [podcast episodes] for my mom on her
phone, and they just sit there. Like she would love S-Town if she listened to it,
but she just can’t seem to get the idea of it somehow.” Because her high school

                                CONCLUSIONS

This qualitative study of regular podcast listeners reveals several emergent U&G.
First, podcast listening supplanted radio listening and television viewing for
many of our focus group participants. The explanations for the shift implicitly
clarify podcast gratifications. Podcast listening won out over radio because of
podcasts’ engaging, always changing content. Listening was preferable to tele-
vision viewing in many in-home situations because podcasts do not command
the visual sense, and they can be listened to on portable devices, thus enabling
people to accomplish housework and other tasks. The television replacement
thread previews our most robust theme of multitasking. Multitasking is broken
into two parts that capture temptation bundling (accomplishing boring or mun-
dane tasks by enjoying podcasts in tandem) and feeding one’s brain by listening
while doing something mindless. In terms of expanding existing gratifications,
focus group participants clarified various avenues of parasocial relationship
development, including a conversational feel to the podcast, frequency of listen-
ing, host sharing of personal information, similarities with hosts, and backchan-
neling options. Finally, the social nature of podcast listening presented
contradictions that expand the preestablished social U&G typology: Because
the medium is relatively new, it created robust niche groups of listeners but
also left behind many who were not willing to give it a try. Put differently, the
social theme is a typology that captures U&G and frustrations about actualized or
missed social opportunities.
   The inductive, qualitative methodology for this study has promoted U&G
typology innovation. A quantitative survey using established or modified typol-
ogies may not have uncovered the nuance to multitasking behaviors, the causes
ascribed to parasocial relationship formation, or ambivalence surrounding the
social aspects of podcast listening. The focus group participants led the research-
ers there. In highlighting some gratifications, podcast listeners have forsaken
others. A 2010 podcast U&G study by McClung and Johnson noted the impor-
tance of library building, which no longer seems relevant. The ubiquity and
relatively low cost of streaming services for music, television, and even films has
made library building an outdated practice. Many of our focus group participants
                                                  PODCASTS AND PRODUCTIVITY                 113

did not want to save or even manage their content, such as Lily (White woman,
42), who had to delete episodes, “otherwise I get super overwhelmed.” With
regard to entertainment gratifications, we were able to drill into more specific
benefits (e.g., portable entertainment, immersive storytelling, and roundtable
discussions that invite listeners in) from our focus group conversations. These
findings suggest that podcasts are appealing primarily because they help listeners
learn more and do more.
   Although the current study has heuristic utility, it was not without its meth-
odological limitations. First, the focus groups were limited in both size and
diversity, especially in terms of respondents’ race or ethnicity as well as their
relatively high level of educational attainment. We also did not assess our
participants’ socioeconomic class or political leanings. These sampling weak-
nesses can inform suggestions for other research. Future podcast studies should
explore potential differences among gratifications sought and obtained by people
from racially and ethnically diverse backgrounds, various educational attainment
levels, political leanings, and socioeconomic classes. Second, use of both face-to
-face and virtual focus groups perhaps means that the nature, flow, and tone of
the discourse shifted as the contexts of those discussions shifted (from mediated
to nonmediated). Despite intentionally keeping the focus groups relatively small
and purposefully providing convenient ways for our respondents to participate,
the qualitative nature of this work along with the methodological decisions for
recruitment and facilitation do ultimately limit the generalizability of the current
findings.
   The findings and processes from this research can be used to make methodo-
logical recommendations for other U&G studies. Qualitative research is the first
step in the classic two-step mixed method many recommend for U&G studies
(e.g., Leung & Zhang, 2016; Ruggiero, 2000; Sundar & Limperos, 2013).10
Specifically asking focus group participants about media displacement in a sche-
dule of questions can help position the medium in question relative to existing
typologies from other media. Furthermore, analyzing the qualitative data in a way
that prioritizes emergent and expanded gratifications is warranted. Our initial
grounded theory analysis yielded 46 themes, too many to be reported on in one
traditional-length manuscript. The story we chose to tell from these data was
grounded in the frequency and depth of mentions but also in U&G innovation:
What newer, prominent gratifications emerged? How do these improve our under-
standing of either podcast listening or gratification typologies more generally?
   The current findings also offer useful information for media practitioners
interested in expanding their podcast listenership. Most broadly, podcasts firmly
signal our shift from a push to a pull media landscape wherein the consumer can

draw upon the customizable, agentic appeal of podcasts to actively assemble their
own listening experience around the realities of daily life. Media practitioners,
then, should concentrate on producing nimble and segmented audio content that
can be consumed on multiple media platforms. Despite shifting from a push to a
pull model, the classic transportational qualities of immersive narratives and
engaging edutainment that have been the hallmark of television, radio and film
for decades still resonate in the mobile and social world of podcasts.
    Another primary implication of these findings is the recognition of divergent
gratifications in the new media sphere: those that command attention and those
that release attention. New developments in media, such as virtual reality, are
focused on engaging the senses in new and absorbing ways. Podcasts, however,
have carved out a niche (or, one might say, recarved an old radio niche) by being
passive and not asking much of listeners. What we see in the robust multitasking
theme is a collection of media gratifications (entertainment, information, enga-
ging storytelling) stacked on a nonmedia gratification (accomplishing physical or
mental work). That stacking is enabled by podcasts being content to stay in the
background. One needs to just lend an ear to receive knowledge, news, and
insight into the human condition from podcasts. Our findings clarify what
podcasts ask of listeners (very little attention in some cases), what it allows
listeners to do (multitask, be on the move, feel connected to others), and what it
encourages listeners to do (check off that to-do list through temptation bundling,
keep expanding their minds). We can thus see that key strengths of the podcast
medium are allowing its promotion of enhanced agency within the medium and
enhanced activity external to the act of listening. This new niche, although
passive in terms of sensory use, allows listeners to actively control their content,
to be their own programmers and brain feeders.
