Abstract
This study investigates how fear, as a central emotional driver, shapes the sharing of misinformation
within MAGA communities on X (formerly Twitter). While prior research has addressed political
polarisation and misinformation, fewer studies explore how affect—especially fear—structures users’
interpretive logics and everyday media practices. This project addresses that gap through a
qualitative case study based on a semi-structured interview with one politically engaged user.
Guided by Lifeworld theory (Habermas) and supported by Affective Publics (Papacharissi), Social
Identity Theory, and Uses and Gratifications theory, the research develops a multidimensional
framework for understanding emotion-driven media engagement.
Findings show that fear doesn’t work as a paralysing trait but rather as an emotional watchtower that,
on the one hand, confirms the in-group identity, legitimises suspicion towards institutions, and, on
the other hand, brings sharing as a civic practice in the front of the stage. The reframing of
misinformation is not that of falsehood, but of emotionally true content in a morally saturated
lifeworld.
The study highlights the limitations of rationalist, fact-based interventions and the necessity of
engaging more fully with the affective infrastructures that animate digital belief systems, identity
construction and political mobilisation..

Introduction

At a time when computer code has become wild and when political divisions grow deeper, platforms
like Facebook and Twitter are as much about emotion as anything. Especially in ideologically
predisposed circles, spaces like X (née Twitter) do not serve merely as conduits for information, but
as affective environments in which users form identities, build affiliations with groups and react to
threats with gut-level panic. The present study examines the role of fear in the communication of
misinformation in MAGA (Make America Great Again) groups on X, and how it is lived,
interpreted and mobilized through the practices of everyday users.
Even as the majority of misinformation literature centers around cognitive bias, ideological
correspondence, or media literacy, there are relatively few studies that take into account the
emotional logics—most notably, fear—that underpins consumption of false or unverifiable
information. The project fills this research gap by reimagining misinformation as an affectively
salient feature grounded in the digital lifeworlds of its users rather than merely as a distortion of fact.
Instead of seeing the consumption of misinformation as passive or irrational, the present work
examines how this content resonates at an affective level, serving to consolidate in-group solidarity
and symbolic action.
Following Lifeworld theory (Habermas, 1981), this study treats digital platforms as spaces in which
meaning is made by individuals in routinised, affective practices. This conceptual kernel is
developed around Papacharissi’s (2015) framework of the Affective Publics and emotional
expression organizes digital publics; Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979) which explains
how in-group validation and perceived threats drive emotional responses; and Uses and
Gratifications which reflects emotionally motivated media use.
Using a qualitative, semi-structured interview with a MAGA-aligned X user, this study investigates
how fear becomes a mode of knowing—how it guides belief, sharing behavior, and collective
interpretation. The aim is not to quantify misinformation, but to understand how it becomes
emotionally powerful and socially productive within affective digital communities. In doing so, the

Literature Review

1. Affective Publics and the Politics of Fear


   Scholars have increasingly acknowledged emotion as central to digital discourse. Papacharissi
    (2015) conceptualises affective publics as networked collectives that mobilise not through
    deliberation but shared emotional intensities. Unlike the rationalist ideals of the Habermasian
    public sphere, affective publics function through resonance, ambient affiliation, and the viral
    circulation of sentiment. Within these spaces, fear serves as a potent cohesive force—it sustains
    group identity, validates dominant narratives, and accelerates the spread of emotionally charged
    content.
Ahmed (2014) complements this view by theorising fear as socially generative. In The Cultural
Politics of Emotion, she argues that fear circulates through bodies and texts, attaching itself
(“sticking”) to figures and symbols of threat. For MAGA communities, fears of institutional betrayal
or cultural decline become structuring affects that define not only political orientation but everyday
engagement with content.




2. The Lifeworld and Subjective Meaning-Making


   Habermas’s (1981) theory of the lifeworld constructs communication as situated in commonly
    shared cultural horizons and assumptions. In digital settings, the lifeworld has become mediated,
    with platforms such as X shaping the backdrop of sorts through which users perceive credibility,
    risk, and community. As Morimoto and Friedland (2010) contend, political media users do not
    only consume media, they experience it. Within this affective lifeworld locus, users experience
    fear which emerges as the lens through which content information is decoded as well as the
    shape of what reality presents.

Under this aspect, misinformation does not get judged by the facticity, but the coherence with
emotional/communal logics. Instead, content that is ostensibly false can still “make sense” when it
is organized within the emotional grammar of a fear-filled lifeworld.


3. Social Identity, Emotion, and Group Solidarity


   Social Identity Theory (Turner & Tajfel, 1979) provides insights into how people derive their
    sense of self from membership in groups. On the internet, expressions of fear like these help
    strengthen and differentiate an in-group. In MAGA’s pegasus brigades, scared-as-you-go stories
    — about election fraud or border vulnerability         do more than simply convince people, they
    affirm identity..


Marwick and Lewis (2017) argue that ‘“Fake news” does not refer to factually inaccurate
information that causes harm,” but rather “to information that is spread with the intention to mislead
in the name of making profit or (more rarely) for political gamesmanship’ (p. 8) 2 and ‘feels true’ in
an emotion-bonded collective. This “emotional truth effect”°is heightened in context of low trust in
institutions and high symbolic belonging — a configuration common to polarised online publics.


4. Uses and Gratifications: Emotional Needs and Media Practice


   Although early U&G work focused on information or entertainment needs, current work has
    embraced emotion as an impetus for media use. Sundar and Limperos (2013) also point out that
    digital affordances allow affective pleasures, and Perks and Turner (2019) note that people often
    seek out content that produces affective stimulation, caress, or validation. For MAGA users such
    as Daniel, the fear-soaked content might serve not just as political commentary but “ as
    emotionally import “stuff” to use for their [sense of belonging through vigilance, their sense of
    communally meaningful self-expression through resistance.
Together, these frameworks demonstrate that misinformation is not merely a matter of distorted facts
or ideological manipulation—it must be understood within the affective and social environments that
give it meaning. Despite growing literature on either emotion or misinformation, few studies
critically examine how fear operates as a lived, emotionally structured logic within politicised digital
lifeworlds. This research directly addresses that gap, investigating how one MAGA-aligned user
                                                                                                         3
experiences, interprets, and circulates fear as a meaningful — if contested — response to perceived
crisis.




Methodology

This study adopts a qualitative, interpretive approach to examine how fear shapes misinformation
sharing within MAGA communities on X (formerly Twitter). Rather than pursuing generalisable
claims or causal explanations, it seeks to uncover the subjective logic, affective motivations, and
meaning-making processes embedded in one user’s digitally mediated lifeworld.
The study is based on Lifeworld theory (Habermas, 1981) that understands everyday communication
to be structured by common cultural assumptions and emotional attitudes. In a platform-dense media
world, spaces like X do more than convey information — they cultivate an emotional frame, an
identity boundary, a moral requirement. This model is grounded in the work of Papacharissi’s (2015)
Affective Publics, which describes how emotions galvanise digital publics; Social Identity Theory
(Tajfel & Turner, 1979), which shows how in-group identity functions affectively; and Uses and
Gratifications (Sundar & Limperos, 2013), providing a theory of affect-driven media use.
We conducted one semi-structured, in-depth interview (n=1) with a purposefully selected participant

                                                                                                  4
who was a 25-year-old US-based postgraduate student, politically conservative, and a
MAGA-embedded active user in X communities. The interview took place on Zoom on May 25,
2025, and lasted 45 minutes. Such a format enabled thematic coherence as well as the flexibility to
track with the participant’s narrative, especially in regard to affective experiences, misbeliefs, and
practices of sharing.
Driven by a hermeneutic approach, the interview was then transcribed literally and analyzed
according to a three-level interpretative strategy: (1) the general experience and affective logic; (2)
linguistic and narrative nuances; (3) subjective themes that emerged. As Kvale and Brinkmann (2009)
argue, qualitative interviews are particularly well suited for comprehending how people make sense
of the convoluted emotional truths of their lives, including their emotional engagements within a
mediatized and politicised environment.
The goal of such a single-case design is not statistical representativeness but depth and context
richness. Ethical approval was gained and informed consent obtained from the participant (identity
anonymised in all of the presentations and the transcripts).



Interpretation

Level 1: Overall Experience and Practice


On a more general level, however, what Daniel does to X (formerly Twitter) should be understood as
an instance of a profoundly affective practice of media usage, where fear is not so much a
debilitating sensation as a constructing principle of perception, action, and identification. Utilizing
Habermas’s idea of the lifeworld, X is not only a medium for communication in Daniel’s life, but
also a social, and symbolic context where meaning is constructed and constantly reproduced.
Daniel says he first got in on the action with X during the 2016 U.S. election, drawn to its
immediacy and what he thought was honesty: “finally, here’s a place where the truth isn’t being
twisted.” This is language that expresses his frustration with institutional media, and implies that X
offers something that is more emotionally compelling — what Uses and Gratifications theory would
refer to as affective utility. He does not seek objective information alone; he seeks a space where
emotional validation, group solidarity, and ideological clarity are available and reinforced.

                                                                                                     5
His day-to-day conversation with X involves talking points such as election integrity, border
security, and free speech. But they are not regarded as merely policy issues — they have existential
significance attached to them. He calls this platform “a frontline in a battle for the soul of the
country,” a description of involvement that is urgent, moral and emotional. This framing echoes the
theory of affective publics developed by Papacharissi: Daniel and his MAGA friends constitute a
network not of shared facts but of shared affect — in particular, fear, urgency, and moral threat.
It is worth noting that Daniel does not see fear as paralysing. “There’s a fear factor there, but “that
fear doesn’t freeze me—it sharpens me,” he says. His is a lifeworld in which emotion is not
irrational; it is clarifying. It flags attention, strengthens group cohesion and rationalises action. His
set of heuristics privileges “internal logic” and community cohesion over institutional authority:
“Credibility … is just a matter of whether it is in line with what the people I trust are seeing as well.”
This position amounts to an extreme version of epistemic collectivism whereby truth is emotionally
and socially co-constituted within an echo-chamber (Murphy, 2018) or technically speaking very
much focusing on the Social Identity Theory.
The ‘tone’°of X is essential to this. The tone, as Daniel puts it, is “intense — angry, frustrated and
occasionally fearful,” but it is a tone he accepts. Instead of casting it as toxic, he pitches it as proof
of authentic civic vigilance: “It’s as if we are all trying to sound the alarm.” The emotional
saturation on the platform isn’t a side effect but a feature — it affirms the gravity of the moment and
sustains his conviction that he is part of a watchful public.
Importantly, misinformation is also rearticulated through this affective logic. “You get fact-checked
on something, that’s even better for me,” Daniel says. Instead, he argues, censorship by official
sources is a sign of yet another hidden truth. This epistemic turn leads to what Morimoto and
Friedland (2010) capture as a splintered lifeworld due to skepticism of dominant systems, where
people orient their sense-making around affective echo and peer validation.
So, for Daniel, being with X is more than its content; it is also emotionally charged and based in his
relational world. For him, fear is more than a feeling; it is a perspective on the world and a driver of
action. X is the mirror and the amplifier at once: It reflects his fears, even as it magnifies them into a
logic of participation, sharing and vigilance.
In sum, the ways that Daniel uses media are existential in the sense that they address basic
emotional and identity requirements. It provides us with information, but also with orientation; not
                                                                                                        6
only with opinions but also with a sense of purpose. By emotionally compelled involvement in X,
Daniel locates his place in a larger battle, not just to expose “the truth,”° but to shield what he
understands to be the heart of America. This affective attachment to it clothing the digital in
something more than a platform; it is also turned into a place where we make homes, emotionally,
and defend them symbolically.


Level 2: Nuances of Practice


The power of the imagery lies in its ability to provide a discursive reality that is built upon the
tension-fraught metaphors to perpetuate a lived experience of watchfulness and moral obligation.
His repetitive insistence that people “wake up,” that “the ground is shifting,” that we are in a “battle
for the soul of this nation,” that we need to “stay alert” turns political life into a continuous state of
emergency. X is framed not as a platform but as a “frontline”—a metaphor that imagines Daniel as a
soldier on the battlefield of a symbolic war. This resonates with Papacharissi’s concept of affective
publics, wherein publics collect around affective energies instead of rational argument.
These metaphors work above and beyond expression — they work on epistemology. As Daniel puts
it, “if something gets fact-checked, then that means that that person can’t be fact-checked true,” he
says, turning suppression into validation. This prying open of epistemic trust suggests a lifeworld
where “truth” is not institutionally constructed but affectively ratified, a conclusion that chimes with
Morimoto and Friedland (2017)’s findings when studying media sense-making under uncertain
conditions.
Anther rhetorical trick is his constant invocation of “they” — the one who doesn’t want you to see
this or is “eroding our rights.” This externalised threat contributes to constructing symbolic
boundaries between an in-group that tells the truth and out-group of censors. This boundary setting,
consistent with Social Identity Theory, fosters belonging and purpose. In this respect, fear is both
the diagnostic and the mobilizing force.
Daniel’s confession also serves an emotional strategic function. He said he reshared content that
was supposed to “jolt” viewers, to make them “stop scrolling” and acknowledge troubling truths. As
in the Uses and Gratifications theory, it is rather the emotional valence and not the factual content
that kindle involvement. Daniel is going for affective disruption, not persuasion.


                                                                                                        7
Affective vocabulary like validation,” “confirmation,” and “resonance” ultimately refers to the
lifeworld of trust as a collectively established one. What’s going on here, then, is not that truth
emanates from institutional credibility, but that it arises from shared emotional logic. What might
seem from outside to be disinformation is, in this affective lifeworld, an affectively coherent
and morally urgent message.


Level 3: Subjective Theme Based on Subjective Experience


At the core of Daniel ’ s story is an underlying theme: Fear as a moral obligation. Far from the
prevailing definition of fear as weakening or irrational, Daniel regards it as ethically indispensable
— a measurement of alertness, preparedness and civic obligation. In this lifeworld, fear is not the
cacophony of emotions. Daniel sees his emotions as evidence of a heightened awareness, not a
weakness. His vow to “wake people up” and “break the spell of complacency,” in fact, places him
in the role of the emotional intermediary, someone who converts affect into awareness. So fear in
this sense is a tool of civic pedagogy: When you share something disturbing or appalling, Daniel
says, you lay the groundwork “for people listening to your more moderate content in order to try
and see more clearly.” This turn the emotional toward conduct and the sharing into morals.
This emotional logic also pushes back against prevailing narratives around misinformation. Daniel
explicitly refutes the suggestion that a fear-based rationale for sharing is a sign of gullibility. He
reframes it as principled defiance: “Sometimes fear is not dysfunction. Sometimes it’s a symptom
of people waking up at last.” In this reversal, fear is evidence of integrity, not of manipulation —
lean-in proof of people who are emotionally attuned to political rot. Here affect is epistemic
authority.
Moreover, Daniel’s distrust of institutional authentication highlights a move away from the truth of
procedure, and towards cohesion of affect. What counts is not what gets officially verified but what
“ feels real ” and what corresponds to his experience of betrayal and loss. Emotion becomes a
diagnostic tool — a means for ferreting out what official narratives suppress. Misinformation, thus,
in his lifeworld, gets realigned with emotional truth as opposed to fact truth.
In other words: while Daniel’s affective worldview isn’t passive, it is strategic, pedagogical, and
collectivist. Fear is not just experienced; it is wielded — as a weapon of shame, as a weapon of


                                                                                                     8
nostalgia and as a fencing weapon of moral alarmism. His acts in X are therefore not merely
expressive; they are performative: they build a lifeworld in which emotion is political, disinformation
is recontextualized, and fear is a shared grammar of meaning and belonging.


Conclusion

This study examined how fear, as an affective experience and social mechanism, influences the
spread of misinformation among MAGA communities on X. Adopting the Lifeworld theory and
qualitative case study approach, the study showed digital emotion environments can function as the
mediations of users’° truth, group identity, and civic duties.
While the study is generalisable to one participant, it offers a dense or thick description of how
political emotions such as fear can work as epistemic and mobilising instruments in digital culture. It
also provides space for future research to examine how other ideological groups emotionally
legitimate contested narratives in other such networked lifeworlds.
In the end, addressing the misinformation crisis requires more than content correction — it requires
an engagement in the affective infrastructures that underpin emotional truths. Fear, it seems, is not a
diversion from reason, but perhaps fear is where belief and participation are produced in digital
publics today.

Key sections from interview & Interview questions
I: When did you first start using X (formerly Twitter), and what initially drew you to this platform?
R: I started using X back then, around like 2016. What drew me to it at first was the immediacy.
News broke there faster than anywhere else. But more than that, it felt like a space where people
were actually speaking their minds, without the filters you’d see on cable news or in the newspapers.
I remember seeing people post things I’d never hear on CNN or The New York Times, and I thought,
finally, here’s a place where the truth isn’t being twisted.
I: What types of topics or content do you usually follow on X, and why do these topics interest you?
R: I mostly follow content related to election integrity, border security, and freedom of speech.
Those are the big ones for me. I also follow a lot of conservative voices—people like Donald Trump
Jr., Marjorie Taylor Greene, and others who aren’t afraid to speak the truth, even if it’s not politically
correct.
I: How would you describe the general tone or emotional atmosphere of political discussions on X?
R: Honestly, it’s intense—very intense. There’s a lot of anger, frustration, and sometimes fear. But
it’s not just random emotion—it’s coming from a place of deep concern. People are genuinely
worried about where the country is going. You can feel it in the way they write, in the memes they
share, and even in the hashtags they use. It’s like we’re all trying to sound the alarm.
Of course, it can get hostile, especially when someone from the other side jumps in. But within the
MAGA community, I’d say there’s a kind of shared urgency. We might be angry, but we’re also
motivated. It’s that feeling that if we don’t speak up now, we might lose the America we grew up
with. Fear plays a role, for sure—but it’s not paralyzing. It’s more like a warning light that keeps
flashing, reminding us we have to stay alert, stay vocal, and keep fighting back against the lies and


                                                                                                        11
the censorship.
I: What kind of content do you typically see being shared by MAGA communities on X, and what
patterns or themes stand out to you?
R: A lot of what I see being shared is either breaking news that the mainstream media won’t cover,
or commentary exposing what’s really going on behind the scenes. There are screenshots of
headlines, video clips from border crossings, footage of crime in Democrat-run cities, and speeches
or interviews that get buried elsewhere. It’s always fast-moving—like the second something happens,
someone from the community is already posting it with analysis or context.
What really stands out to me is how many of these posts try to connect dots. Like, it’s not just about
one news item—it’s showing how it fits into a larger pattern of government overreach, globalist
influence, or cultural decay. It’s about telling a bigger story, and making sure people know there’s a
fight going on, even if the mainstream wants to act like everything’s fine.
I: When encountering posts about social or political threats (e.g., immigration issues, election fraud
allegations), how do you typically feel, and why do you think you have these emotional reactions?
R: I usually feel a mix of anger and fear—sometimes it hits hard. Like when I see videos of people
just walking across the southern border, or stories about ballots showing up late with no explanation,
my gut reaction is this deep sense that the country is slipping away. It’s not just frustration—it’s fear
that the systems we used to trust are breaking down, or worse, being weaponized.
And I think the reason I feel that way is because it’s personal. I grew up believing in a certain idea of
America—fairness, freedom, law and order. But now it feels like all of that is being eroded. I see
people getting away with crimes because of politics. I see our own voices being silenced for asking
questions. It’s like the ground is shifting under our feet, and you don’t know who’s really in control
anymore.
I: When you see extreme or potentially false information on X, what influences whether you consider
it believable or not?
R: That’s a good question. Honestly, it depends less on whether the information has a “source” in the
traditional sense and more on whether it resonates with what I’ve already seen or felt happening
around me. If something sounds extreme, I don’t immediately reject it—because these days, the
so-called “mainstream” version of truth has failed us so many times.
I don’t trust blue check marks or official media links automatically. In fact, when something gets
                                                                                                       12
fact-checked or labeled as “misleading,” that actually raises my interest. It makes me wonder: why
are they trying so hard to suppress this? What are they afraid of?
What really matters is the context—who’s sharing it, what their past content has been like, and
whether it aligns with patterns I’ve noticed myself. If someone posts something that connects dots
between different events or explains something I’ve been suspicious about, I’m more likely to
believe it—even if it hasn’t been “verified.”
I: If you decide to share or repost content, what kinds of emotional responses do you expect or hope
to evoke in others, and why?
R: When I repost something, I’m usually trying to wake people up. I want them to feel what I
felt—whether that’s anger, urgency, or even fear. Not fear in a debilitating sense, but the kind that
shakes you from your comfort zone. I feel this way just now, when comfort itself feels dangerous.
My hope is that someone who sees my posts pauses just for a second and has to ask
themselves, Wait, is this really happening? That disruption — that’s what I’m trying to get to. I
don’t post for the sake of posting anything. I share to trigger a reaction, to create a spark, to push
someone to start asking questions they weren’t asking before.
And to be honest, sometimes I share things that are hard to look at—videos of violence at the border,
or stories about political corruption—because I think people need to confront reality, not hide from it.
We’ve been lulled into complacency for too long, and I see sharing as a way to break that spell. If
someone feels uncomfortable after reading what I post, then maybe that’s the first step toward them
seeing things more clearly.
I: Have you ever experienced moments of anxiety or fear because of something you encountered on
X? If so, what happened and how did it affect you?
R: Yes, definitely. One moment that stands out was during the 2020 election. I remember late at
night, seeing post after post from people showing videos of ballot dumps, strange activity at polling
places, and numbers changing in real time. I wasn’t watching the mainstream networks—they’d
already called things—but on X, people were sounding alarms, and it felt like something was
seriously wrong.
That kind of fear stays with you. It made me more vigilant, more skeptical of anything that comes
from “official” sources. It also made me more active—I started posting more, joining spaces,
following accounts that dug deeper into the data. I felt this strong need to not just process my fear but
                                                                                                       13
to turn it into something—to warn others, to stay ahead of what’s coming.
I: When you notice feelings of worry or fear spreading among others you follow on X, how does that
shared emotional atmosphere influence your own views or actions?
R: It definitely has an impact. When I see people I follow—especially those I trust—start to post in a
more urgent or anxious tone, it reinforces my sense that something serious is happening. It’s like this
collective radar, and when everyone’s picking up the same signal, I pay attention. I take that
seriously.
I think that atmosphere also builds trust. When you see others showing fear—not weakness, but real
concern—it humanizes them. It makes me feel like we’re in this together, not just scrolling alone.
That kind of emotional connection makes you listen more, believe more, and act faster.
I: What other important insights or experiences related to fear, misinformation, and your use of X do
you think I should understand, which we haven’t discussed yet?
R: One thing I’d add is that for people like me, X isn’t just a place where we passively scroll through
content. It’s become a kind of frontline—not in the physical sense, but in terms of information and
awareness. Fear plays a role, yes, but it’s not just about being afraid of what might happen. It’s about
being afraid that no one’s listening, that the truth is being buried, and that the people who are
supposed to protect our rights are actually the ones eroding them.
And about misinformation—I think it’s important to understand that that word gets used a lot to shut
