          Common questions
• Q: Do I need to use all of the survey questions for the report?
• A: You do NOT need to use all of the survey questions for the report. Select the
  important ones in the written report or leave them in the appendix.


• Q: Is it okay to report insignificant results?
• A: It is OKAY to report insignificant results that do not support your hypotheses (e.g.,
  you do NOT detect any difference in satisfaction between two gender groups).
  However, you should provide reasoning why it is still interesting to report null results.
  (see next page for further explanation)
            Hypothesis testing: Dealing with non-significance

  Seeing lots of non-significant (NS) results? Don’t panic, it’s just a normal part of research. Continue exploring the
                                  data and develop new hypotheses to test if needed.

                      Do include a mix of significant and non-significant findings in your report.


• You will still get the full Results interpretation marks as long as you interpret your NS
   output correctly.

• You do get extra Discussion/Critical analysis points for explaining potential reasons for
   your NS findings.
        Example: Writing up non-significant results


“We conducted an independent-samples t-test to test H2. There is a no significant

difference in purchase intention between physically active and non-active customers,

t(38) = -1.397, p > .05. This can be further confirmed by checking the mean figures for

the two sample groups, Mactive = 4.39, Mnon-active= 4.41. These results do not support

H2. We therefore do not reject the null hypothesis.”
          Common questions
• Q: Why is my p-value in ANOVA smaller than 0.05, but I don’t see any pair comparisons
  with a p-value smaller than 0.05?
• A: In ANOVA, this is usually caused by the small sample size. Refer to the next question
  (e.g., borderline p-values) for potential solutions.
• Q: What to do with borderline significant p-value (e.g., p = 0.071).
• A: By default, we assume a significance level of α = 0.05 for hypothesis tests. However,
  due to small sample sizes, you might find that many p-values in your SPSS outputs are
  borderline (e.g., p = 0.071). There are two ways to deal with them:
   – Interpret the results assuming α = 0.05; you can indicate that you do not reject the
      null hypothesis at the 95% significance level; however, it is significant at the 90%
      significance level.
   – You can explicitly set α to be a slightly larger value (e.g., α = 0.15). In this case, you
      can say that you reject the null hypothesis at the 85% significance level.

Types of Data
What characterizes data?
• Variable: an attribute whose value can change. A variable is the opposite of a constant.
• A case (or observation) consists of all the observed variables that belong to an object such
  as a customer, a company or a country.
• Item: usually refers to a survey question put to a respondent

                                         Constructs
     • Construct: a variable that is not directly observable. Examples:
        − Happiness
        − Trust
        − Satisfaction
     • Not all constructs (or construct measures) are alike:
        − Reflective constructs versus formative constructs
        − Single- versus multi-item constructs
     • Developing constructs requires using scale/index development procedures

            Clean data - outliers
Outliers are values situated far from all the other observations. There are three types:
Outliers due to data collection or entry errors
Exceptionally high or low values that are a part of reality
Combinations of values that are exceptionally rare


Should we delete or retain outliers?
If they are data collection or entry errors: yes (or try to look up the correct values)
If they are not, retain and describe the impact of these outliers.


To detect outliers in the data, use boxplots or calculate z-values (          ).
Rule of thumb: z < -3 or z > 3 are likely to be outliers.                                  34
