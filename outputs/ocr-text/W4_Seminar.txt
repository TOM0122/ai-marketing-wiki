            Measurement
Assigning numbers or other symbols to characteristics of objects according to certain pre-specified rules.
• One-to-one correspondence between number and characteristics
• Rules for assigning numbers should be standardized (everyone uses the same scale to measure the same
  object should give you the same answer)
• Four scale types are commonly used: nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio
             Measurement Scaling
Four levels of measurement scales
1. Nominal
2. Ordinal
3. Interval
4. Ratio

The level of measurement scales determine
❑ Information level obtained
 • Nominal scales measure the lowest information level, ratio scales provide the greatest amount of
   information
❑ Level of statistical analysis
 • Nominal scales allow simple analyses, ratio scales allow more sophisticated analyses               8

             Interval scales
• Each value on the scale is assumed to be an equal distance apart
• We can interpret the magnitude of the differences in values directly
• The location of the zero point is not fixed but arbitrary (e.g., temperature in Celsius) → no absolute “0”
• Descriptors are sometimes placed evenly apart on the questionnaire to indicate interval

            Single vs. Multiple Item Scales
Decision to use single vs. multiple-item depends on:
• Type of variables to be measured (concrete vs. abstract)
• Survey method
• Analysis method



Compared to multiple item scales, single item scales …
• make measurement more efficient and the questionnaire shorter
• reduce the quality of your measures
                       Single vs. Multiple Item Scales
     Single item scales – ex. purchase intention



     Multiple item scales – ex. brand loyalty
      Q. To what extent do you agree with the following statements? (1 = extremely disagree, 7 = extremely agree)
      a. After I get used to a brand, I don’t like to switch.
      b. I see myself as a brand loyal person.
      c. I feel really committed to the brands I buy.
      d. Even though certain products are available in a number of different brands, I always tend to buy the same brand.
      e. I prefer the brand I always buy instead of trying something new I am not sure about.

Multiple item scales - reliability
              True score model: XO = XT + ES + ER

                   −XO = the observed score
                   −XT = the true score
                   −ES = systematic error
                   −ER = random error

              Reliability
              •   The degree to which what we measure is free from random error (ER=0)
              Validity
              •   Whether we are measuring what we want to measure (ES=0)
              Generalizability
              •   The extent to which one can generalize from the observations at hand to
                  a universe

            Multiple item scales - reliability
3 key factors to assess reliability
❑ Internal consistency
• Can be used when using multiple items to measure a construct
• If these measures relate strongly and positively to one another there is a considerable degree of internal
  consistency
• Cronbach’s alpha – The coefficient ranges from 0 to 1. A coefficient level should be at least ≥ 0.60 in
  order to be considered reliable.
❑ Stability of the measurement (test-retest reliability)
• The extent to which a scale produces consistent results if repeated measurements are made over time
• Only can be assessed for variables that are relatively stable over time
❑ Inter-rater reliability
• Assess the reliability of secondary data or qualitative data

          Functions of a Questionnaire
• Translates research objectives into specific questions
• Standardizes questions so that each respondent is asked the same way
• A well-designed questionnaire fosters cooperation and encourages respondents to
  complete the survey

          How to phrase? (principles)
1. Avoid using extreme words. e.g., all, always, best, ever, every, most, never.
2. Avoid loaded questions. e.g. We are happy when you like programs on Channel 7 and
   sad when you dislike programs on Channel 7. Let us know what you think of our
   programs.
3. Avoid using ambiguous words (be as specific as possible). e.g. How satisfied are you
   with our branch?
4. Avoid double barrelled questions. e.g. How satisfied are you with your pay and job
   conditions?
5. Avoid broad questions. i.e. What is your attitude about the link between the war on
   terrorism and the democrats decrying socialism?
6. Avoid leading questions. e.g. How do you generally spend your free time, watching
   television or what?
         How to phrase? (principles)
7. Avoid using abbreviations or jargons.
8. Avoid questions which are hard to answer. e.g., Do you recall any commercials from the
   program that you watched last night on Channel 7.
9. Avoid using wrong/ambiguous scales.
10. Avoid questions which make respondents inclined to answer what they think they
    should have done. e.g. Many people are washing their clothes less often because of
    concerns for the environment. How has your concern for the environment affected the
    way you wash clothes?

                Wash less     No Change     Wash more
            How to Organize Questions
1. Start with screening questions or filter questions
• Determines whether the respondent fits the researcher’s selection criteria.
Have you stayed at /reviewed for a hotel in the last one year?


2. Warm-up questions
• Use easy questions to show the respondent that the survey is easy to complete, and generate respondent
  interest in the survey
How often do you travel each year?
            How to Organize Questions
3. Use transition statements (include “skip” questions)
• Notifies respondents that the subject or question format will change. Sudden shifts in topics are to be
  avoided as they tend to confuse respondents.
In the following questions, we would like to ask you questions on how you rate the services
of a hotel.


4. Section the questions for the main research objectives
• Avoid including too many questions in one section. If possible (e.g., online survey), present each section of
  questions or each question on a separate page to reduce fatigue.
             How to Organize Questions
5. Leave personal (demographic) and possibly offensive questions until last
• By the end of the questionnaire the respondent has developed some trust and confidence in the
  researcher and/or study.
Age, gender, income, BMI, marital status, etc.


6. Interdependencies: ask questions that could be affected by other questions first
Example:
1)Do you like Coca-Cola?
2)Do you know how much sugar Coca-Cola contains?

             Pretest a Questionnaire
How to pre-test
▪ Test the reliability of measurement items
▪ Test the readability of questions
▪ Measure the time required to complete the questionnaire
▪ Test interdependencies (filter-questions)

Some questions to be asked during pre-testing stage
▪ Can the questionnaire format be followed by the interviewer
▪ Does the questionnaire flow naturally and conversationally
▪ Are the questions clear and easy to understand
▪ Can the respondents answer the questions easily
▪ Which alternative forms of questions work best
