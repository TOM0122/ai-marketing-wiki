        Types of Sampling
• Probability sampling
 – Every element of the known population has a known, nonzero probability of being
   selected.
 – Sample is representative. Thus, findings are projectable to the entire population
 – Sampling error can be computed, and inferential statistics can be calculated


• Nonprobability sampling
 – Cannot make inferences about the general population
 – Generally used for qualitative research
 —convenience, judgment, quota, snowball

          Simple Random Sampling

❖ Each element in the population has an equal chance to be selected.


 ❖ Advantage: Good generalizability and unbiased results


 ❖ Disadvantage: difficult to obtain a complete, current and accurate listing of the target population
   elements (i.e., sampling frame). Each sampling unit needs to be identified.


 ❖ Application: random-digit-dialing (RDD) technique to generate random telephone numbers.

       Stratified Sampling
❖ Reduce sampling error by splitting the population into groups
❖ Process:
 ✓ Identify classification factor
 ✓ Split population by classification factor
 ✓ Draw random (or systematic) samples from each group
❖ Results in homogeneous-within, heterogeneous between sample groups

A sample of convenient elements

• Least expensive
• Least time consuming

• Not representative → hard to generalize findings
         Judgmental Sampling (purposive)
The elements/subjects are selected based on the judgment of the researcher
• Examples: test markets, expert witness in the court, subjects in the focus group
• Popular in qualitative studies


Advantages
• Low cost and still less time consuming than most of the techniques
Disadvantages
• Results depend on the judgment of the researcher
• No guarantee for a representative sample

        Sample Sizes
❖ Intuition: Increasing the sample size of a randomly selected sample helps reduce
  sampling error.
❖ The marginal benefit of increasing the sample sizes becomes smaller as the sample
  becomes larger. An increase from n=40 to n=60 (i.e., + 50%) is more beneficial than
  from n=1,000 to n=1,020 (i.e., + 2% ).
❖ The sample size required is (almost) unrelated to the size of the population.

         Determining Acceptable Sample Sizes (1)
Collecting more data provides greater reliability but
does not improve validity.


However, the cost of greater samples is high.
• Web surveys $0,35-$10,00 per additional
respondent
• Face-to-face $50 -$200 per additional respondent
• Larger samples take longer time to collect – may not
support quick decision-making
• Could hurt validity in extreme cases

        Margin of Error (E) and Confidence Interval (CI)

Margin of Error (E) = maximum expected difference between the true population value
and the sample estimate — due to random sampling.
The margin of error is influenced by
 ✓ Confidence level (e.g., z-value)
 ✓ Population standard deviation (σ)
 ✓ Sample size (n)


Confidence Interval (CI) = The range within which the true population value likely falls.
               CI = Point Estimate ± Margin of Error

Nonresponse characterizes surveys and polls. Few participate.

the distribution” – we are not obtaining representative samples.
