 What Is a Confidence Interval?

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of values we believe contains the true value.



It helps generalize from a sample to the whole population.


CI tells us: "If we repeated this many times, this range would contain the truth most of
the time."


Intuition: Like a target, the CI is the area where most of your arrows (estimates) land.

            What Is a Z-score in a Confidence Interval?
The z-score reflects how many standard errors we go out from the estimate to build the interval.
It depends on the confidence level you want:
 • 90% confidence → z = 1.645
 • 95% confidence → z = 1.96
 • 99% confidence → z = 2.576
Higher confidence → wider interval → larger z-score
Z-score assumes a normal distribution (central limit theorem helps this for large samples)
            Summary: What Goes into a Confidence
            Interval?
1. Point estimate: What you got from your sample (mean or proportion).
2. Standard error (SE): How much your estimate is expected to vary from sample to sample.
3. Z-score: Reflects your desired confidence level (e.g., 95%).


                                          CI = Point Estimate ± z × SE


• Narrow CI → larger sample, lower variability, or lower confidence
• Wide CI → small sample, high variability, or high confidence

            Vietnam Ad Campaign
From the Vietnam ride-hailing ad campaign:
 • Estimated proportion p = 0.58
 • Target MoE = ±4.8% = 0.048
 • Confidence level = 95% → z = 1.96
Apply formula: n = (1.96² × 0.58 × 0.42) / 0.048² ≈ 405
Conclusion: Need at least 405 responses for ±4.8% MoE at 95% confidence
The actual survey had n = 400 → close to ideal, results are reliable
