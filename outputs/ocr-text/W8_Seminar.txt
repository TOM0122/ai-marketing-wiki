             Independent Samples t-Test Cheat Sheet
             (Levene’s Test → Independent Samples t-Test)
1.    Step 1. Levene’s Test: Test for equality of variances
     a) This is to test the “equal variances” assumption. The p-value in the Independent Samples t-Test
          depends on whether this assumption is satisfied.
     b) Null Hypothesis: H0: σ12 = σ22;
     c) Alternative Hypothesis: H0: σ12 ≠ σ22


2.    Step 2. Main test: Independent Samples t-Test
     a) Null Hypothesis: H0 : μ1 = μ2; Alternative Hypothesis: H1 : μ1 ≠ μ2
     b) Use the p-value based on whether equal variances assumed or not
     c) Statistical decision using p-value
     d) Interpretation (e.g., is one average higher than the other one?)
             Example
H1: There is a difference in the likelihood to recommend the
Oddjob Airways (NPS) between different flight purpose
(business vs. private). We hypothesize that the those who fly
for business are more likely to recommend Oddjob Airways to
a friend or colleague.


• Statistical test: independent samples t-test
• Null hypothesis: μbusiness = μprivate, where μ = population
  mean of NPS.
• Alternative hypothesis: μbusiness ≠ μprivate

             ANOVA
❑ Compare the means of three or more groups

Assumption
• The dependent variable is measured on an interval or ratio scale.
• The observations are independent.


Examples
• Do light, medium, and heavy users differ in their monthly disposable income?
• Do customers across four segments differ in their attitude towards a certain brand?​
• Is there a significant difference in hours spent on the internet daily among the users of TikTok,
  Instagram, and Rednote?
                                                                                                      18
             Why ANOVA?

• When comparing 3+ groups, you might think to do several t-tests between pairs.
• But this inflates the Type I error rate (false positives).
Example:
• Suppose you’re comparing 4 customer segments (e.g., Gen Z, Millennials, Gen X, Boomers) on
  supplement satisfaction.
• The number of comparisons grow s rapidly. There are 6 pairwise comparisons (4C2 = 6).
• Each t-test at α = 0.05 has a 5% chance of error. The overall error rate increases with each test:
                     f𝑎𝑚𝑖𝑙𝑦𝑤𝑖𝑠𝑒 𝐸𝑟𝑟𝑜𝑟 𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑒 = 1 − 1 − 𝛼 𝑘 = 1 − 0.95 6 = 0.265
• So, 26.5% chance of making at least one false discovery!

           Types of ANOVA
• One-way ANOVA: Compare means across 3 or more groups based on one independent variable (factor).
   – Tests if group means differ significantly.
   – Uses one categorical IV. For instance, age group, region, or user type.
   – Example in DS Research: Compare satisfaction with a new supplement across 3 age groups: Young
      adults, middle-aged, and seniors.
   – Assumptions:
   1. Normal distribution within groups;
   2. Homogeneity of variances;
   3. Independence of observations
• Two-way ANOVA: Examine the effect of two independent variables (factors) on one outcome, and
  whether they interact. The assumptions are the same as in One-Way ANOVA
   – Example: Factor 1 = age group; Factor 2 = gender; Interaction effect = does age influence
     satisfaction differently for men and women?

            One-way ANOVA
How to report the results of a one-way ANOVA test?
Example
In this study, we hypothesized that overall satisfaction with the price-performance ratio of Oddjob Airways
differs based on traveler status. The results of a one-way ANOVA test indicate that traveler status affects
overall satisfaction with the price-performance (F(2, 1062) = 9.963, p < .001). Therefore, our hypothesis was
supported.
Further Hochberg post-hoc analyses reveal that blue-status customers are more satisfied than silver-status
customers in terms of price-performance ratio (Mblue = 4.47 vs. Msilver = 4.03, p < .001). Additionally, blue-
status customers express higher satisfaction compared to gold-status customers in the price-performance
ratio (Mblue = 4.47 vs. Mgold = 3.99, p < .01). Other group comparisons do not yield significant differences.
