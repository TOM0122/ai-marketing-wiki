                 01 Project overview
                    An outline of the research project
This report presents data collected from primary and secondary research on a multi-year
project titled   'Economic geographies of transition: beyond Australian automotive
production'. The research project was funded by the Australia Research Council (ARC)
under the ‘Discovery Early Career Research Award (DECRA)’ scheme.

The DECRA project (DE180100492) examined the spatial and economic aspects of
restructuring unfolding in Australia’s automotive sector, specifically so-called secondary,
‘aftermarket services’. The focus on aftermarket services was deliberate. In October 2017,
the last new passenger cars made in Australia rolled off assembly lines in GM-Holden’s
Elizabeth factory on the outskirts of Adelaide. Over the previous 12 months, Ford
(Broadmeadows, Geelong) and Toyota (Altona) also ceased making new cars domestically,
ending a high-value manufacturing industry that had provided well-paid, unionised jobs and
been a source of national pride for close to a century (see McDonagh 2011).

A range of factors explained the closure of new car manufacturing in Australia. These
factors included Australia being a small, geographically isolated market; a dramatic shift in
consumer preferences from large family sedans to SUVs and dual-cab utes; the
comparatively high costs of manufacturing vehicles domestically; increased competition
from newly industrialised countries, and Australia’s removal of import tariffs and
government assistance that had spurred a classic ‘branch-plant’ spatial arrangement to
new car production.

Mirroring the United States, in the post-war period Australia’s settlement pattern became
characterised by detached housing in suburban areas (urban sprawl) closely associated
with new road infrastructure and rising car dependency. By 2020, there were roughly 775
vehicles per 1,000 residents in Australia, a 31% increase in ownership rates from 1990.

While new production has ended, costing around 30,000 full-time equivalent jobs,
Australia’s high levels of car dependency continue to support a large, spatially dispersed
aftermarket economy. This aftermarket is underpinned by technical services and products
that maintain the safe and reliable use of vehicles alongside their second-hand value
(Warren and Gibson 2021). Importantly, while car manufacturing and assembly are spatially
mobile and increasingly fragmented across global production networks (GPNs),
aftermarket services require geographical proximity to cities and towns where car
ownership levels and dependency are high.

Aftermarket commercial services are connected to the production and consumption of
motor vehicles, forming a considerable economic enterprise in their own right (Warren and
Gibson 2021). Aftermarket activities include specialised services and products such as
maintenance and repair (M&R), aftermarket accessories, spare parts, materials recycling,
insurance and financial products, and the sale of user-generated data, such as that now
captured via telematics technology.

                                                                                          2
Responding to a lack of research on product aftermarkets, including for Australia’s
automotive sector, the DECRA Project aimed to develop an evidence base and analytical
framework to offer new insights into aspects of industrial change that have major
implications for the national economy and a spatially mobile society.

Integrating insights from more than 60 semi-structured interviews, quantitative data sets,
and ethnography in auto servicing shops, the project traced structural changes in
aftermarket industries and documented transition experiences among place-based auto
businesses. Conceptually, a relational economic geography framework was deployed to
help interpret emerging technical and competitive dynamics among auto repair and
maintenance businesses. These place-based firms service an increasingly complex and
information-rich consumer product manufactured by globally-networked brands and
multinational corporations.

Through an immersive research process, the project was also able to examine the changing
skill requirements among a traditionally male-dominated ‘blue-collar’ workforce. In light of
struggles identified within downstream auto businesses to fill vacant positions with suitably
skilled and experienced workers, the report concludes by making three recommendations
for industry groups and policy-makers. These recommendations are intended to assist in
reproducing a skilled and valued workforce well-equipped for the future. In addition to this
report, a series of academic publications are connected to the research and can be
accessed via the Reference List contained on page 50.

Importantly, the data sets presented in this report help to illustrate how the causes of
industrial transformation involve a combination of factors. In the auto sector, the multi-
scale factors driving change include the effects of government laws and policies (e.g. US
Clean Air Act 1999; Inflation Reduction Act 2022), evolving technologies and intellectual
property rights, changes to production and retailing practices, the reliance on cars for
mobile everyday life, and struggles to reproduce a workforce skilled at keeping cars in
reliable working order. A key objective of the report is to offer policy-relevant insights as
new forms of regulation emerge globally around the sharing of technical and proprietary
information among industry actors; so-called ‘right-to-repair’ laws.

The chief investigator on the project was Dr Andrew Warren, an economic and labour
geographer based in the Australian Centre for Culture, Environment, Society, and Space
(ACCESS) at the University of Wollongong (UOW). A broader groups of researchers also
contributed to the project, including:

                     02 Shifting Gears
                        The restructuring of Australia’s
                        auto sector
Restructuring has been a near-permanent feature of advanced industrial economies since
the early 1970s (Warren 2019). The auto sector has been an emblematic example of such
transformation. Writing about the deindustrialisation of America’s economy four decades
ago, Bluestone and Harrison (1982: 114) pointed out that “no single product with its extensive
linkages to other economic sectors… has ever so dominated the imagination of the
population, or base of a national economy as did the car”. The auto sector is deeply
interconnected with a range of other commercial functions and activities: road and
infrastructure works; urban planning and suburbanised housing developments; corporate
finance and personal credit markets; legislative and regulatory approaches spanning trade
policy to environmental protection; personal mobility and lifestyles; regional economic
development; technological and organisational change, employment and skilled labour
markets (Pavlínek 2020; Pollard et al. 2021; Warren and Gibson 2021). In the 1960s, across
advanced economies, up to one in six jobs were indirectly connected to the automobile
(Bluestone and Harrison 1982).

By the 1970s, cars had become a quintessential product of globalising capitalism. With a drive
for greater efficiency and returns on investment, auto brands increasingly segmented and
dispersed production among tiers of original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) and
subcontractors (Sturgeon et a. 2008; Rutherford and Holmes 2014).

Then, in 1990s, amendments to the U.S. Clean Air Act required all cars sold in the country to
be fitted with On-Board Diagnostic (OBD) technology. The goal was to reduce urban air
pollution by improving fuel efficiency and lowering exhaust emissions. More sophisticated
generation II OBD technologies were mandated from 1996 onwards. The EU followed suit in
1999 and regulated the compulsory installation of diagnostic technologies (Directive
1999/96/EC, Art. 4).

Meanwhile, Australia lagged. Amendments to the Motor Vehicles Standards Act (1989) only
occurred in 2006. U.S. and EU regulation precipitated a range of innovations in auto design
and manufacturing. Whilst couched in an environmental agenda, technological changes
were accelerated by automakers in the 2000s and 2010s as part of targeted marketing
strategies aimed at selling more new vehicles (Vinsel 2019).

Also important to the story of industrial restructuring was the changing preferences of
consumers, a reminder that end-users co-create successful brands. In markets such as
Australia large family sedans were top-sellers for several decades e.g. the Ford Falcon,
Holden Commodore, and Toyota Camry. These were also designs being manufactured
onshore. Yet, by the 2010s, an increasing portion of the new car market favoured small
hatchbacks, SUVs, and 4WDs. Hatchbacks offered greater fuel economy and convenience for
inner-city driving. SUVs and 4WDs, including dual-cab utes, offered a raised drive height and
greater flexibility in use for ‘trades’ workers and busy families alike.


                                                                                            4
By the 2020s, the highest-selling car models in Australia were the Toyota Hilux and Ford
Ranger, both diesel-powered 4WDs with significant towing and off-road capabilities. This
collection of factors help to explain the restructuring of the auto industrial complex, which
took on a distinctive character in Australia (cf. Warren 2019).

As auto plants were shuttered in Melbourne, Geelong, and Adelaide, Australia became an
import-only car market. As mentioned, the significance of the plant closures included the
loss of 30,000 well-paid jobs across industrial, working-class communities (Beer 2018). More
broadly, the nation’s industrial capacity was also negatively impacted.

Multinational automakers pointed to a range of factors in deciding to divest from Australian
branch plant production: comparatively high production costs; competition among global
firms; constrained economies of scale in a small, geographically isolated region; falling levels
of state support; low import tariffs amid free trade agreements with major auto exporting
nations (e.g. China, Japan, South Korea), and an increasingly segmented new car market.

A Federal Senate Economics Committee Inquiry (2015) preceded the plant closures and
focused on mapping the future of a post-production ‘auto economy’. The inquiry noted that
“the downstream sectors will account for around 95 per cent of all activity within the
Australian automotive industry after 2017”(SECI 2015: 4). By February 2023, post-production
activities such as car retailing (sales), servicing and repair, accessories and spare parts trade
contributed around 350,000 jobs nationwide. Auto repair and maintenance was the single
largest industry, comprising 153,600 jobs or 44% of auto industry employment (ABS 2023).

High vehicle ownership rates, rapid technological change, and the inability to self-service an
elaborate machine have deepened the reliance of Australian consumers on buying after-
sales services from specialised businesses. With the end of new car production, historically
celebrated in the national economic imaginary, downstream activities such as maintenance
and repair were recognised as crucial to sustaining the working condition of motor vehicles.
Today, cars are products not only crucial to personal mobility but also the ability for many
households to access life opportunities (Warren and Gibson 2023).

Contrasting macroscale indicators pointing to growing aggregate demand for downstream
services, a granular view suggested local auto businesses were facing a specific set of
challenges. During the Senate Inquiry, the Motor Trades Association of Australia (MTAA 2015:
11) reported that downstream auto businesses were enduring “a period of unprecedented
restructuring and economic upheaval”. Brand-aligned dealerships were facing declining
margins on new car sales and strict franchise conditions pushed by powerful automakers.
Spatially dispersed independent mechanic shops, comprising 90% of the business count,
were losing aftermarket share and closing at an alarming rate. Meanwhile, industry groups
reported a looming skills shortage among an undervalued workforce that faced new training
and upskilling requirements.

Across hierarchical production networks, established automakers continued to wield
multidirectional power (Sturgeon et al. 2008; Pavlinek 2020). Such power infiltrated post-
production services and auto shops via the control of retailing practices and flows of
informational content associated with a complex, evolving consumer product.

The Senate Inquiry’s final report noted a structurally uneven downstream auto sector and
emphasised the need for exploring strategies to effectively regulate business practices.
Research presented in this report commenced amid ongoing industry upheaval and
uncertainty about the future of auto-related aftermarket services.

    Australia’s New Car Market
In 2023, new car sales in Australia topped 1.21 million. This
represented a sales record, surpassing the previous high of 1.18
million new cars exchanged in 2017.
Some noticeable trends in the new car market included:

Shifting Geographies of Production: In 2023, China overtook
South Korea to become the third-highest source country for new
vehicle imports in Australia, now only behind Japan and Thailand.
Market Competition: Sales data highlights the significant
competition for market share in Australia. Several less established
marques and models (e.g. MG, Tesla, Isuzu) are capturing market
share from established brands (e.g. Toyota, Hyundai). This trend in
brand diversification indicates that Australian consumers are
seeking out greater value for money.
A Record Year for EV sales: More than 87k fully electric cars were
sold in Australia during 2023. This volume of EVs equated to a
record 7.2% of overall sales.
Electrification: Including battery electrics (BEVs), plug-in hybrids
(PHEV) and hybrids (HEV), nearly 200k electrified cars were sold
across Australia in 2023. This volume of electrified vehicle sales
represented 16.5% of total new sales nationally.
The Tesla Brand: For the first time, Tesla entered the top 10 for
sales by brand. Tesla continued to have Australia’s two highest-
selling electrified cars: the Model Y and Model 3. These two models
achieved more than 46k sales in 2023.

                What are aftermarkets?
 An aftermarket includes the secondary commercial transactions linked to the
 consumption and ongoing circulation of primary commodities. For durable
 consumer products, such as cars, computers, personal electronics, equipment, and
 household appliances, a significant amount of economic activity now occurs in
 aftermarkets. Such commercial activity includes specialised, technical services
 (e.g. repair of a faulty transmission) and brand-specific products (e.g. spare parts
 that require calibration).
 Expanding design complexity, intellectual property claims, regulatory conditions,
 financial obligations, safety, and sustainability concerns mean that consumers
 must now engage the services of aftermarket businesses rather than conduct DIY
 repairs or maintenance as was common in an earlier era of automobility.

Auto mechanic workshops are a prime example of an aftermarket business. Australia’s auto
repair and maintenance industry (ANZSIC S9419) includes a range of aftermarket services
that are directly linked to motor vehicles and their day-to-day use, including:

   General mechanical repairs and logbook servicing (maintenance)
   Smash repair (panel beating, spray painting)
   Cooling system and radiator repairs
   Engine repairs and reconditioning work
   Exhaust system or muffler repairs
   Gearbox and transmission repairs
   Commercial and fleet vehicle repairs and maintenance

The tables, figures, and maps presented in Dataset 1 explored some of the key dynamics of
Australia’s auto markets: both the primary ‘new’ car market and downstream ‘aftermarket’
activities. Table 1 illustrates the popularity of different auto brands. In 2023, Toyota enjoyed
the highest share of the new car market with 17.7% of all sales. This figure represented a
significant drop from 21% in 2022. Future results will give a better indication of any trend.
Following Toyota, Mazda enjoyed a new market share of 8.2% with Ford (7.2%) in third place,
boosted by sales from two models: the Ranger and Everest.

When compared to other advanced, car-dependent economies, Australia now has one of the
world’s most segmented markets. New car sales per brand are roughly 14 times greater in the
U.S. and eight times more concentrated in the UK. Market diversification has created
opportunities and challenges for car dealerships and aftermarket businesses.

The research behind this report found that very few franchise dealerships (outside of Toyota
dealers) can now sustain profitability as single-brand outfits. The vast majority of Australia’s
3,500+ dealerships now offer 3-5 different marques to ensure consumer foot traffic.
Likewise, the research found that for independent mechanic shops, a diversifying market
requires becoming familiar with how to fix a bigger range of brand-related technologies with
design variations, different driving platforms, and idiosyncrasies.

Equally significant for aftermarket services is that SUVs and 4WDs now dominate Australia’s
car market. Figures 3 and 4 show that consumers have a strong preference for larger dual
cab utes and high-riding SUVs with no family sedan in the top 10 for sales in 2023. By
contrast, just a decade ago nearly 60% of all new car sales were family-oriented passenger
vehicles. In 2023, 56% of new car sales in Australia were SUVs, up from 22.9% in 2012.

Of note, sales of BEVs, PHEVs and HEVs continue to grow in Australia, with Tesla breaking into
the top 10 for sales in 2023. While the uptake of electric technologies is expanding, evidence
collected from the research underscores the reality that a full transition of the nation’s fleet
from internal combustion engines (ICE) to an EV-dominant market will take decades. The
speed of the transition is explained by a range of factors, further explored in Section 5 of this
report.

Finally, a series of useful insights emerge from the data regarding aftermarket services.
Figures 13a and 13b were compiled from research with five franchise dealerships (2019 to
2023). These dealerships were based in the city of Wollongong, Australia, 70 kms south of
Sydney. Interviews with dealership managers and staff showed that while new car sales
contributed around 50% to business revenue, margins on new cars averaged just 3%. By
contrast, aftermarket maintenance and repair services averaged 25% of annual revenue
but had a net profit margin of 19%. In total, the contribution of aftermarket services to
dealership profits averaged nearly 60%. While the new car market shifted during 2021/2022
following the effects of the global pandemic, supply chain disruptions, and inflation, by mid-
2023 business trends had returned to near pre-COVID norms.

                          Summary: Automobiles as
                          complex, evolving technologies
The figures, tables, and maps presented in Dataset 2 share insights into the evolving,
complex technologies that comprise modern automobiles. The technological architecture
of cars shapes aftermarket services directly, including fault diagnosis, repair processes,
maintenance scheduling, new parts sourcing, access to brand-specific equipment,
workforce training, and upskilling. With cars being the primary mode of transport for most
Australian households, a breakdown or delay in a repair has a major impact on mobility and
daily lives. As outlined in Figure 14, motor vehicles are now the world’s most high-tech
consumer product.

However, despite the integration of complex, networked and evolving digital technologies,
cars remain prone to unexpected breakdowns. Vehicles require ongoing expenditure on
repair and maintenance services to ensure good working order. Importantly, as indicated in
Figures 15 and 16, the increasing complexity of cars also corresponds with an increasing
number of annual recalls across different marques, models, and variants. For example,
between 2019 and 2023, Australian motorists experienced an average of 175 car-based
recalls per annum, as registered with the Department of Transport and Infrastructure. This
level of product recall represented a 111% increase compared to the period 2009-2013 and a
370% increase compared to the period 1993-1997.

Whilst the overall size of Australia’s motor vehicle fleet increased by 29% between 2010 and
2023, the more dramatic increase in the rate of vehicle recalls suggests that the increasing
technological complexity of cars is correlated to an increasing number of faults requiring
remediation by qualified technicians. Repair work related to a vehicle recall is usually
covered by the manufacturer but often represents a safety or disruption risk to a consumer
using a car with an identified fault.

As visually represented by Maps 3 and 4 (page 20), an increasing number of vehicles are now
exported from newly industrialised countries such as China, Thailand, India, the Czech
Republic, Slovakia, Mexico, and Brazil. In 2023 China became the world’s largest
manufacturer and exporter of finished automobiles on a unit basis and sat second to
Germany in terms of export value (US$). When focused on export markets, Germany
remains at the top of the global value chain but is likely to experience increased competition
for market share and earnings amid the transition to electrified cars.

The global geography of auto manufacturing is a significant factor for local aftermarket
businesses and consumers who will require access to brand-specific technical information,
proprietary software, and equipment to access and deliver affordable, timely repair and
maintenance services. Indeed, the research found that in 2023 a growing proportion of
repair jobs in mechanic shops (20-25%) required access to brand-specific technical data,
diagnostics and/or design schematics. This proportion of jobs is double the 10% figure
formally reported by the Federal Government in 2021.

                                                                                          23
Following the maps, Figures 17 and 18 provide further insights into the expanding
technological complexity of modern automobiles. Figure 17 shows the growing revenues
earned by semiconductor manufacturers from the sale of microchips to auto brands and
OEMs. Indeed, the CAGR in the trade between automakers and ‘chip’ manufacturers was
10.5% between 2013 and 2023. Automakers purchased US$54 billion worth of microchips in
2023 alone with most analysts predicting strong forward growth as digital technologies,
including artificial intelligence(AI) become an ever more important feature in selling
vehicles.

Figure 18 illustrates the contribution of electronic componentry to the overall cost of
manufacturing a motor vehicle. The material costs from electronic componentry have
grown from 18.2% of overall production in 2000 to more than 40% in 2022.

The infographic on page 22 describes perhaps the most controversial technological
development unfolding within the automotive sector: telematics. The ability to capture,
transmit, interpret, and on-sell user-generated data has become the backbone of the tech
sector. Through everyday driving practices, motorists are now deeply implicated in the
generation of an enormous volume of data related to driving habits, geographical mobility,
vehicle performance, and fault diagnosis.

There are a range of ways in which accurate data on car use and performance can be
beneficial for motorists and carmakers. These potential benefits include the ability to more
efficiently repair vehicles and improve overall reliability.

Equally, there are considerations for regulators and policy-makers regarding the privacy
and rights of consumers who may not consent to having aspects of their personal lives
shared with data brokers, product developers, or advertisers. In addition, the increased
digital connectivity of vehicles is also raising cyber-security concerns as the risk of having
personal data stolen by hackers grows.

Perhaps more than any other mass-produced consumer technology, cars illustrate the
increasingly complex and stretched-out relationships between economic actors: global
brands, OEMs/manufacturers, shippers, retailers, credit providers, repairers, parts
wholesalers and distributors, market regulators, policy-makers, and final consumers who
rely on their car to go about busy, mobile lives.

The research behind this report shows that there is still much to resolve around the
informational politics of complex, technology-rich products.

                    The consumer-product relationship

 Consumption = the exchange of goods and services to meet needs and
wants. Crucially, these needs and wants are socially mediated and change
                           over time and space.
Australia has one of the highest rates of car ownership, per capita fuel consumption, and
vehicle-related travel distance, of any country. The factors that explain Australia’s car
dependency are multiple, including urban settlement patterns, government policy
(industrial, transport, taxation), cultural views that link cars to ideas of freedom, personal
choice, comfort and flexibility, and economic factors spanning job creation to financial
investments in road infrastructure.

For many Australian households and families, car ownership has enabled a mobile lifestyle.
Cars support trips to places of work, small business and commercial activities, attendance at
social or community events, family holidays, and road trips. Increasingly, however, car
ownership is a major cost burden. The costs of running a car relate to credit-backed
purchasing, insurance premiums, and fuel costs. Motor vehicles also continue to make a
significant contribution to Australia’s annual greenhouse gas emissions, air and noise
pollution.

While expensive and environmentally damaging, car ownership often remains socially
necessary. Without access to a vehicle, the majority of Australians would be unable to easily
access places of work, education, health and childcare, social activities, grocery stores, and
other essential services. For these reasons, cars remain important to access what Polllard et
al. (2023) refer to as ‘life opportunities’.

What the research project set out to investigate was the modern-day experience of car use
and ownership, including how consumers access aftermarket servicing and repairs. Hence, in
this section of the report, results are presented that highlight key trends, consumer-facing
problems, and potential policy mechanisms that may support competitive aftermarket auto
industries while improving the overall ownership experience for Australians.

In addition to interview material, participant observation at shops, and ABS data, this section
also draws from a comparative analysis conducted of emergent policy responses in
Australia, the U.S. and the EU. Such policy measures aim to address issues of inequitable and
unfair access to proprietary information for delivering aftermarket services on increasingly
complex vehicles. Such legislative and regulatory frameworks are commonly referred to as
‘Right-to-Repair’ bills.

When the research commenced in 2019, Australia did not have formal, mandatory ‘Right-to-
Repair’ legislation covering aftermarket servicing of consumer goods. However, the situation
shifted mid-way through the project, providing an opportunity to examine if new legislative
actions taken by Government had their intended impact.

Consumer Insights: What are the servicing needs of EVs,
Plug-in hybrid vehicles (PHEVs) and hybrid vehicles?
As the share of electric-powered vehicles increases, consumer-related questions have
focused on the aftermarket servicing and repair needs of EVs e.g. battery packs, motors,
suspension, braking systems, remote software updates, and over-the-air servicing.

Leaving aside petrol-related savings, some reports, including by Yale’s School of the
Environment, suggest that scheduled maintenance and repair costs on EVs will be reduced
by up to 40% over traditional ICE vehicles. Cost-savings are explained by the reduced need
for the expert labour of mechanics and the ability to utilise networked digital technologies
for remote, over-the-air software updates, diagnostic reports, and rapid identification of
servicing needs. Broadly speaking, consumers may visit mechanic shops less often and be
empowered to handle system updates from home or organise maintenance only when
instructed by a vehicle’s ‘smart’ operating systems.

On the flipside, however, other evidence uncovered through the course of the research
project indicated that any savings made in scheduled servicing are offset by higher upfront
costs (retail), lower resale value, and increased aftermarket costs related to more frequent
replacement of tyres, malfunctioning battery packs, expensive parts/componentry, and
higher insurance premiums.

For example, several interviews with auto technicians who had worked on EVs uncovered
expensive issues with traditionally ‘minor’ repairs such as replacing headlights and shock
absorbers. In these instances, the time to complete repairs, including recalibration, added
40-50% to the cost for similar work on an ICE vehicle. Likewise, according to technicians the
additional weight of EVs over ICE equivalents (typically 20% heavier) and instant torque
means the lifespan of tyres is reduced by up to a quarter.

                      Summary: The consumer-product
                      relationship
The figures, tables, participant quotes, and descriptions presented in Dataset 3 illustrate
changing patterns of motor vehicle use among Australian consumers. Focusing on the
consumer-product relationship, the data shows that many Australians continue to rely on
access to a motor vehicle for their day-to-day lives, though with some major changes
regarding aspects of car usage for work-related trips.

Figures 19 and 20 reveal trends in car use related to accessing places of paid work. The
proportion of work-related trips involving use of a motor vehicle, as a driver or passenger,
declined sharply from 67.3% in 2016 to 56.3% at the 2021 Census. The biggest explanatory
factor in the decline of car-related trips to work was the COVID-19 pandemic. The
pandemic caused major disruptions to the typical geographical patterns of paid work.
Throughout 2024, many Australians continued to work from home (WFH) during part of
their working week. Despite some employers mandating a return to centralised workplaces
(e.g. CBD-based office spaces), many employees in service industries continue to seek out
opportunities for hybrid and flexible patterns of work that incorporate work from home.

Of note, public transport usage between 2016 and 2021 trended downwards, also likely due
to social distancing norms following public health measures during the pandemic response
of 2020 and 2021. Increasing public transport usage remains a major challenge for policy-
makers, particularly as growth areas in Australia’s largest cities tend to follow greenfield
land releases accompanied by urban sprawl. Many large residential developments are
dislocated from public transport infrastructure such as rail lines with the result being a
stubborn car dependency for personal mobility.

Big shifts in the geography of paid work and thus work-related travel are further illustrated
in Figure 20 on page 26. The figure reveals how 21% of Australian workers engaged in WFH
on Census day in 2021, a substantial rise from the three previous Census periods where less
than 5% of employees worked from home.

Despite recent changes in car use for work-related travel, the series of quotes from
research participants, shown on page 27, underscore the close and enduring relationship
between Australians and their cars. As a young parent and customer at an independent
mechanic shop explained when asked about their car use: “There’s just no way I can get to
places I need to go without my car”.

Following the series of quotes from participants in the study, Maps 5 and 6 provide a visual
representation of car registrations across Australia’s states and territories. The level of
vehicle registrations closely maps to the population size of states/territories and the
settlement patterns of major cities and towns.

Table 2 highlights growth in the proportion of electric-powered vehicles, as registered by
state and territory. As of mid-2024, the ACT (5.73%) and NSW (3.73%) had the highest rates
of EV ownership. NSW alone now has more 211k registered battery electric and plug-in
hybrid vehicles, increasing from 81k in 2021. Meanwhile, Tasmania (2.16%) and the Northern
Territory (2.74%) have the lowest rates of uptake in electrified vehicles.

The insights provided on page 30 and via Table 3 shed light on a much-publicised debate
over the costs of EV ownership relative to conventional ICE-powered vehicles. Some initial
studies, including by researchers at Stanford University, have suggested that EV servicing
costs will be considerably lower than comparable ICE models. Such studies emphasise the
ability to conduct over-the-air software updates, systems checks, and diagnostic tests,
negating the need to take a vehicle to a service centre.

However, there is also emerging evidence that the story of aftermarket cost savings for
consumers is not so clear-cut.

While the general servicing costs of EVs may be lower compared to ICE cars, consumers
may find they are paying higher insurance premiums, replacing tyres more frequently (EVs
are ~20% heavier than ICE cars, on average), and requiring access to specialised
technicians when it comes to recalibrating electrical parts and componentry.

Another important consideration for many car buyers is resale or second-hand value. Again,
EVs significantly underperform in terms of value depreciation compared to traditional ICE
vehicles. While still a relatively ‘new’ consumer technology, the value of EVs 3-4 years old
averages 39% less than similar ICE models. The following scenario is instructive.

If two families are in the market for a mid-sized SUV in the range of $70,000, the electric
model could be worth up to $19,800 less than the ICE variant after 3-4 years. At 5-6 years of
age, current resale data suggests that EVs hold just a quarter of their original new car value.
This compares to ICE vehicles that retain around 68% of their value after 5-6 years.

Table 4 on page 31 then outlines four broad, general categories of car maintenance and
repair identified through the research: 1. product recalls, 2. warranty repairs, 3. breakdown
repairs, and 4. logbook servicing, equivalent to scheduled maintenance. These four
categories of maintenance and repair reflect both the service needs of car owners
alongside the specific type of work provided by auto aftermarket businesses.

While product recalls and warranty repair costs are usually covered by manufacturers,
breakdown repairs and scheduled logbook servicing are usually the responsibility of car
owners/users.

For consumers, breakdowns are particularly disruptive as they are often unexpected,
meaning any resulting disruption is not planned for. Scheduled maintenance, however, can
be planned around personal schedules and family routines. In this way, repair work can have
very different spatial and temporal characteristics compared to maintenance.

In terms of consumer behaviour, rights and responsibilities, the timeline on page 32
accounts for some of the significant recent changes in Australia regarding auto
aftermarket services and so-called ‘right-to-repair’ laws. In 2016, a voluntary code of
practice was introduced nationally to encourage automakers to share service-relevant
information with independent mechanic shops.

However, by 2020 only 3 of the 15 highest-selling brands in Australia were openly sharing
information with independent shops, such as technical service bulletins (TSBs), calibration
procedures, onboard diagnostics, and software upgrades. In response, the federal
government passed legislation that resulted in the Motor Vehicle Information Scheme
coming into effect on 1 July 2022.

The legislation now requires automakers selling cars to consumers in Australia to grant
independents access to the same codified information as their dealerships. The technical
information can be accessed by independents for “a price (scheme price) that does not
exceed the fair market value of the information” (MVIS 2022, p. 11).

Regardless, the research heard from several independent shop owners and managers who
described long wait times for repair-related information, including incomplete schematics,
poor technical support, and high one-off fees that added substantial costs for small
businesses and their customers. While viewed as a major step forward, some in the auto
industry argued the regulation was proving “less effective than we had hoped for”
(Manager, Independent auto shop).

Focusing on transport-related expenditure among Australian households, Figures 21 to 24
over pages 33-34 offer insights into the growing costs of running a vehicle, including
servicing car loans, paying for fuel, and insurance. For example, the average car loan
repayment increased by 65.3% between 2020 and 2024, which is an annualised increase of
13.0%.

By the second quarter of 2024, the average weekly spend on a car loan stood at $211.80 per
week, or roughly 46% of a household’s weekly spend on transport. Likewise, fuel costs
increased 67.1% (to $101.40 by Q2 of 2024) while insurance expenses had risen 46.8% since
2020 to reach $43.60 per week.

By contrast, the rate of increase for aftermarket auto servicing, including scheduled
maintenance (e.g. logbook servicing) and running repairs, was a more modest 23.3%. When
annualised, the increase of 4.7% p.a. is in close alignment with the rise of the consumer
price index (CPI).

At the other end of the spectrum, expenditure on public transport declined by -5.0%
between 2020 and 2024, averaging $21.0 per week.

Overall, by mid-2024 the average Australian household was spending a record $459 each
week on transport, equivalent to 17.0% of median household income.

A final collection of empirical insights derived from the research project focus on the skilled
workforce in Australia providing auto-related maintenance and repair services. The post-
production auto workforce not only helps to sustain the safe, reliable working order of
vehicles but also the mobility of Australians, more broadly.

Two of the key workforce-related questions the research aimed to help answer were:

1. What are the training requirements and key skills needed by workers who maintain and
repair complex modern vehicles?

2. What are the barriers to developing a skilled and valued auto maintenance and repair
workforce?

In seeking to shed light on these questions, the research integrated both qualitative and
quantitative data.

Qualitative methods encompassed several rounds of interviews spanning auto mechanics,
customer care staff, shop managers, and dealer principals. Participant observation was also
conducted in dealer service centres and independent shops to capture in-depth insights
into the work of auto maintenance and repair.

Quantitative data was collected from ABS Census and custom data requests, alongside
publicly available data sets published by industry groups such as the Motor Trades’
Association Australia (MTAA) and Australian Automobile Association (AAA).

This section on workforce dynamics concludes with three recommendations, emerging
from the wider research project. These recommendations aim to assist industry
stakeholders, policy-makers, and training institutions to further develop a future workforce
that is well-equipped to service complex vehicles, which help to keep Australians spatially
mobile.

A crucial point the report emphasises is that reproducing a suitably trained and skilled
workforce will require a clear understanding of the labour process behind maintaining and
repairing new model motor vehicles, and for the skills of workers in such downstream
industries to be appropriately valued.

                     Summary & Policy Recommendations:
                     Workforce insights & struggles over skills

According to the National Skills Commission (NSC, 2021, p. 1) of 34 auto-related
occupations assessed for the Skills Priority List (SPL), “62% were found to be in shortage,
compared with 19% for all occupations”. Across occupations found to have significant
shortages, there was a strong concentration in downstream auto M&R services, including
mechanics, auto electricians, panel beaters, trimmers and painters.

During the project, the research uncovered a consistent story of local auto servicing shops
experiencing problems recruiting suitably qualified and experienced staff to fill job
vacancies. These results align closely to insights from labour market analysis conducted by
the NSC and Motor Trades Association Australia (MTAA). For example, the NSC (2021)
reported that auto-sector employers filled just 47% of advertised vacancies in 2020/21,
with an average of 1.8 suitable applicants per job vacancy. Alarmingly, 30% of employers
received no suitable applicants for advertised positions. By comparison, employers outside
the auto sector reported to the NSC that 61% of vacancies were filled in 2021 (NCS 2021)

In terms of workforce skills and expectations, the NSC (2021) found that 90% of employers
required applicants to hold a formal qualification, typically a trade-level qualification (such
as a Certificate III or IV). Where qualifications were specified, employers received an
average of 3.8 qualified applicants per vacancy, but 50% of these were deemed unsuitable.

Overall, the main reasons why applicants were found unsuitable when applying for an
automotive-related vacancy were twofold. First, 58% of employers noted applicants had a
lack of general experience in the occupation. Second, 54% of employers reported to the
NSC (2021, p. 4) that applicants had a “lack of qualifications”. Other reasons given for the
worker shortage included “insufficient specific skills or experience (39%)” and “performing
poorly at one or more stages of the recruitment process (38%)” (NCE 2021, p. 4).

In addition, there are discrepancies between urban and rural/regional areas when it comes
to skill shortages in Australia’s auto sector. For instance, employers based in regional areas
filled just 45% of their vacancies and received an average of 1.4 suitable applicants per
vacancy.

The above figures compare to auto employers in urban locations who filled 50% of
vacancies and had 2.2 suitable applicants per vacancy. Across all Australian employers,
there was an average of 12.8 job applicants per vacancy with 2.9 suitable applicants per
advertised position (NSC 2021).



                                                                                            45
The labour market data indicates that Australia’s auto sector is facing a workforce crisis,
indicated by a relatively low proportion of suitably qualified and experienced applicants for
vacant jobs. If the skills shortage becomes ingrained, there will be significant negative
impacts to both auto businesses that keep motor vehicles in good working order and
consumers who rely on them for mobility and commerce.

In response to skill shortages and employer difficulties filling job vacancies, auto industry
groups and associations have lobbied governments to shift policies and increase skilled
migration quotas. The broad aim of such strategies is to attract a higher number of
migrants with the technical skills and relevant experience to fill shortages in key industries.

In contrast to a focus on labour migration, this ARC-funded research makes three
recommendations that focus on developing a suitably skilled and valued local workforce.
The three recommendations are intended to support the development of a workforce to
support the growth of the downstream auto sector and the safe mobility of all Australians.

Recommendation one is for auto groups, government policy-makers, and educational
institutions (e.g. TAFE, secondary schools) to collectively revisit how younger Australians
are being recruited and trained for trade-based and technical careers, specifically in
auto-related industries. This recommendation responds to the finding that the work of
mechanics, auto electricians, panel beaters, and painters has fundamentally transformed in
the last decade. Such transformation is due to the combined effects of evolving auto
technologies, new environmental regulations (e.g. emissions reduction targets), and
consumer habits (e.g. favouring larger SUVs/4WDs).

Equally important is that skills are not merely attributes that an employer buys and deploys.
Skills are context- and information-rich capacities developed in an ongoing social process.
This social process includes learning in workplaces via on-the-job training and mentoring,
alongside formal training institutions such as Australia’s TAFE and university systems.




                                                                                                46
Recommendation two is for a new evidence-based national strategy to more accurately
communicate what a job in the auto sector now involves. Such a marketing and educational
campaign should include highlighting the career benefits of working in a fast-paced area of
the economy, and the skills needed to work on/with automobiles and consumers who rely
on them for their mobile daily lives. Emerging from interviews with auto shop employees,
the project found that current routes into the sector poorly represent the importance of
learning about aspects of automotive software, reading design schematics, fault
diagnostics, creative problem-solving, and abstract analytical thinking.

It is no longer accurate to narrowly define auto work as ‘blue-collar’. A changing labour
process - the social and technical aspects of work - necessitates shifting popular
perceptions about what a job in the auto sector entails. A closer understanding of the work
will also assist with better preparing automotive workers, including studying subjects such
as maths and engineering at secondary school level.

Recommendation three is for employers and local businesses in the auto sector to adopt a
strong internal focus on fostering workplace cultures that more equally value the skills mix
within their workforce. Such a skills mix includes, for example, technical roles in repair and
maintenance alongside customer care roles that are increasingly vital to the profitability
and competitiveness of auto shops that need to maintain a high car count (through-put)
and high levels of customer satisfaction, including through digital channels.

Recommendation three emerges from participant observation in auto shops, which
revealed how workers in dealerships and independent auto shops often had uneven
experiences of skills recognition related to managers’ perceptions of their ‘replaceability’
and contributions to the operations of a business.

In addition to recognising their processual qualities, skills are both a key mechanism by
which workers express agency in workplaces and labour markets (Warren and Gibson
2023). Skills also underpin the success of auto businesses that must focus on servicing a
complex technical product and serving customers who rely on their vehicles for daily life.

                  07 The Road Ahead
             Forward-looking insights on Australia’s
             post-production auto sector
As complex manufactured products, modern automobiles are both expensive and durable.
Conventional market economics and government industrial policy tend to focus attention
on the point of production where well-paid, unionised jobs are concentrated. Downstream
aspects of product use, maintenance and repair tend to be comparatively neglected (Fine
2002). Going beyond a partial view of commodity relations, this ARC-funded research
explicitly focused on Australia’s post-production automotive sector, as the nation entered a
new era of import-only automobility.

Amid climate change and emissions reduction goals, there is a growing focus among
researchers, policy-makers, businesses, and institutions on second-hand markets, reuse
practices, repairability and recycling to improve material circularity. The goal is to reduce
wastefulness and create more opportunities for sustainable and inclusive economic
growth.

In the auto sector, aftermarket activities now underpin a process of extended profit-
making and accumulation entangled with the rhythms of mobile, day-to-day life (Pollard et
al. 2021). This project has emphasised five key dynamics shaping Australia’s downstream
auto sector, which require future research and policy attention.

The five inter-related dynamics identified as being crucial to downstream automotive
services include:

1. the relationship between a dispersed and diversified primary market and locally
embedded aftermarket services such as auto maintenance and repair.

2. the influence of evolving technologies on value capture in downstream activities such as
parts, data harvesting, servicing, and repair.

3. the politics of informational sharing and control, which will require a carefully
coordinated regulatory approach.

4. consumer dependency on automobiles for accessing life opportunities in cities and
regions, and

5. institutional struggles over the reproduction of critical skills needed in downstream
industries such as maintenance and repair. An expert workforce is required to diagnose
faults, repair complex problems (breakdowns), and maintain working order.

The results of the research project clearly show that amid the influence of complex and
evolving technologies on branded consumer products, downstream services are an arena
increasingly defined by contestation and struggle between different competing actors -
corporate firms, brand-aligned dealerships/retailers, and independent service providers.


                                                                                            48
Modern cars are now ostensibly open-ended vehicles that support profit-seeking activities
over a longer, consumptive cycle. Through entanglements with everyday life cars can be
theorised as unfinished commodities (things of value) where opportunities to realise
surplus value are calculated, coordinated, and competed over once they are being used.

As right-to-repair movements and regulating institutions target aspects of maintenance
and repair, spare parts trade, and data transfer the informational control over complex and
evolving products is a reminder that certain actors remain powerful forces in lucrative
aftermarket domains.

The post-production and post-sale lives of global commodities encapsulate different
relations and modes of power. The dispersed production networks typical of many
manufactured consumer goods are structured by hierarchical power relations (Sturgeon
et al. 2008; Coe and Yeung 2015).

This research has found that the power of global brands and OEMs is not restricted to the
sphere of production or retailing. Manufacturing firms can exert power over the post-sale
lives of products that become the legal property of others.

Moreover, despite recent regulatory efforts in Australia to support the open and fair sharing
of technical and design information (e.g. MVIS 2022 legislation), struggles continue for
many independent service providers in the timely and affordable access to informational
content crucial for servicing and repair.

Such informational content includes access to software codes, design schematics,
technical service bulletins, recalibration and fix procedures. Manufacturers can charge
technological rents for technical information that is often incomplete, unsupported and
slow in arriving to independent shops and customers.

A final message from this research is that researchers and policy-makers need to combine
insights across production, distribution and consumption-related activities. Such insights
are increasingly valuable in a world where consumer goods are mass-produced via
stretched-out spatial networks and where access to affordable post-sale servicing is vital to
ensure that people’s social lives are not further disrupted.

Ultimately, incorporating activities such as maintenance and repair into economic analyses
can help better account for the different experiences of owning, using, working on, and
profiting from commodities that continue to circulate in time and space long after they
leave the factory gates, retail store or distribution centre.
